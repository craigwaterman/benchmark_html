I broke up with my debit card

Posted in personal finance, tips and tricks by cassidynoga

I’m so bad at breakups. I feel SO BAD and avoid it and cry and altogether hate the process. This applies even if I’m “breaking up” with a company.

Today, I cancelled my debit card and DIDN’T CRY! I’m so proud of myself. Here’s the deal: I didn’t pick this debit card in the first place. It was the refund option the University gave me back when I was a wee little freshman. There were no fees as long as you were a student, and I got my money right away. Cool beans, worked for 18 year old me.Â After that, I didn’t see a reason to change it.

Until now. I’ll graduate in a couple months (!!!) and I don’t NEED a debit card. I haven’t used it in months – preferring always to use my cash back credit card – and I do have a debit card with my credit union if something goes terribly wrong. So, adios, debit card!

If I could do it all over again, I probably wouldn’t ever get that card. It was alright, I guess, but I would recommend just having the money deposited in a normal checking account so that it all stays in one place.

I would also tell my 18 year old self to get a credit card ASAP. My personal preference is to always make sure it has some kind of benefit. My first card had a deposit (it kind of sucked), but I still got cash back. It was a great way to build up a year’s worth of credit history… but that card also had a fee, so I cancelled it after year 1. I then switched to the Amazon card – no fee, 3% cash back on Amazon purchases… it works great for me. I’d like to get the Bank of America Travel Rewards Card, but that’ll probably come later (like, once I have a job).

My biggest piece of advice to anyone getting a credit card is to DO YOUR RESEARCH. Seriously, there are so many options now and they all have different benefits or disadvantages. Figure out what YOU want, how you’ll use the card, and do some math – will you spend enough to capitalize on the perks the card advertises? If so, check your credit score and go for it.

3 things I love right now

Posted in conversation by cassidynoga

I’ve tried doing monthly favorites. It just doesn’t work for me. I’m SO BAD about doing the whole photo collage and honestly, by the time the end of the month rolls around, I’ve forgotten what I’ve liked all month.

Instead I’m going to try random posts with just a few things that I love RIGHT NOW. I might even do these weekly or something because I love a lot of things and like sharing. Ready? Here we go.

 

1. my mom’s chicken enchiladas. I used to say that shredded chicken tacos were my fave food ever, and they kind of still are… but I also really like enchiladas, and the chicken is the same… and I kind of like them better as enchiladas (how many times can I say the word enchiladas in one post? LOTS OF TIMES). I’ll do a recipe post on it soon because they’re pretty easy but also kind of complicated. It’s a process at least, so I’ll share it later on. Seriously though, I could eat these all the time.

2. The Happy Hour with Jamie Ivey (podcast). YOU GUYS. I’d heard about this podcast before but it’s a Christian podcast and I’m not super religious… anyway, Emily from Jones Design CompanyÂ was on two episodes and so I went and listened… and now I’m HOOKED. The podcast is so good that I sent a text to my Little this morning telling her she HAD to go and listen. It’s so much like listening to a couple of your best girlfriends chat (and not being able to say anything, but trust me, you’ll wish you could call these ladies up and gab for a while). It’s not in-your-face with religion, but it’s JUST ENOUGH to inspire you to be better about life. I have a fewÂ girlfriends in real life that I talk Jesus with sometimes and it’s perfect and fun and I always leave feeling so happy… this is just like that. Jamie brings on different ladies every week (two weeks? I’m listening to old episodes right now so I’m not sure what the current schedule is) and it’s just FUN.

3. watercolors. I’m seriously like a five year old with my creative habits… basically my life is like, oh let’s do THIS craft and then THIS craft and those aren’t done yet but let’s start THIS ONE TOO. I’m sure my parents love it. (Not.) Anyway, I’ve been trying to figure out watercolors for some time now because they look so good when artsy people do them… and mine look terrible. TURNS OUT there are actual rules you should follow… like using watercolor paper. It’s a DUH thing but I didn’t. I tried to use printer paper and just no. It doesn’t work. It works OKAY on the greeting cards I make (that’s another post I plan to put up at some point…) but seriously, watercolor paper is so much better. I’ve read that the 140 lb paper is the best but it’s all I’ve used at this point so I don’t have any comparison.

 

So I read somewhere that ALL blog posts should have an image (because otherwise, you can’t link them to pinterest!), but I’m realllllly new at Illustrator, so this one is going to be imageless for a while. Maybe I’ll come back and add something in once I finish Emily’s class on Illustrator (which will prob be in my next “love” post).

five years forward

Posted in conversation by cassidynoga

A question thatÂ has haunted me recently is the famous (or perhaps,Â infamous) “where do you see yourself in five years?” As a kid, I could tell you exactly what I planned to do, right down to the college I wanted to go to (which ironically was TAMUCC, my current university, so while I ended up at my childhood dream uni, it wasn’t myÂ teenageÂ dream university. Sigh). I threw everything out of whack when I decided to go to a middle school out of my district, which led to going to a high school also out of my district. I’m pretty sure that my answer to the five-year question was a safe bet – five years past high school would either have me in college or grad school (which, you know, is accurate).

The past few years have been the tricky part, and it’s only gotten harder as I’ve gotten closer to the end of education. I’m not sure where I’ll be in five years. I honestly haveÂ no clueÂ where I’ll be. I’m fairly certain that I won’t still live with my parents (something I’m sure we’re all grateful for), but I can’t say with even a grain of certainty at which city – or state – I’ll be in. I do know that I’ll be 27, but I’m currently single… is five years enough for me to meet someone and fall in love? Maybe, but maybe not. Will I have my dream job by then? Is it even possible to move up the ranks that fast? Will my dream jobÂ nowÂ even be my dream job once I try it out?

I’ve proven time and time again that I can’t predict the future. I can’t – and don’t want to – set deadlines and limits for myself. Where do I see myself in five years? I don’t.

It’s not that I don’t set goals, or have dreams, or aspire to be or do something. It’s not that I don’tÂ wantÂ to do things with my life, and it’s certainly not that I don’t work towards those things. I just don’t look towards the future and decide that a certain accomplishment will happen by a certain time.

Sure, I have age goals. I want to have a book published by the time I’m 30. An old boyfriend got me hooked on the idea of visiting all 50 states by the time I’m 30. (Thirty’s going to be a big year!) I’d like to retire (from a corporate life) early and spend my time doing things I love – even if those things involve working. I want to be financially sound as early as possible – 27 would be great! Is that realistic? We’ll see, but I’m not going to set myself up for failure by saying that I want it within five years.

I think it’s important to have goals and dreams, but I don’t think it’s necessary to set definite limits and predict where you’ll be in a certain number of years. I live my life by the motto that everything happens for a reason, and if it’s meant to be, it will find a way. So many things in my life have happened because I was in the right place at the right time, and had the qualifications to jump at the opportunity. I didn’t plan to go to the middle school I chose. I didn’t plan to join BPA (Business Professionals of America) or fall in love with accounting. I didn’t plan to work during college, yet I loved the job at Harte that almost literally fell into my lap. To be fair, I didn’t even plan to go to grad school… it just happened because my grades were good and I needed extra credits before sitting for the CPA exam and I didn’t want to get a real job.

Maybe in five years, I’ll be living on the beach somewhere (says the girl who already lives in a sort-of beach town…). Maybe I’ll be living in a big city. Maybe I’ll have gotten started in real estate. Maybe I’ll be making art. Maybe I’ll have things figured out.

It’s fun to dream about five years from now, but thinking about it too seriously stresses me out big time. I’m just going to let it happen as it wants, and figure things out as they come.

faking normal {book review}

Posted in books by cassidynoga

 

I picked FAKING NORMAL up at a Barnes & Noble in Chicago. I kept picking it up, reading a random bit, putting it down… and then being drawn back to the book to pick it up again. That’s how I pick books – either by the cover (of course I judge books by their covers… that’s what they’re for), or by reading either the first few lines or some random page. If I want to know more, I buy it. It’s only failed me once or twice in all my book buying years.

Anyway, FAKING NORMAL was captivating, not in a “this book is so suspenseful” way (or, not in the bookstore, at least), but in the “I seriously cannot stop reading this book”. It came home with me. I started reading it on the airplane, and let me tell you, there’s a serious tissue warning for this book. You should be able to gather that from the back of the book or from the first few pages (it starts at a funeral, come on), so that’s not a spoiler. If anything, it’s an incentive to read the book.

It’s a beautiful book. It deals with loss and heartache, with friendship and romance. It deals with growing up and not knowing how to act or think orÂ be.Â It deals with strange habits and coping methods. It deals with healing.

I love it. I’m going to put together a list of my favorite books of all time – that’ll be out sometime soon – and this one is on it, for sure. I don’t even have to think about that. I literally started the book over as soon as I finished it, and when I got home (because remember, reading on airplanes… which also meant flight attendants asking if I was okay…), I started looking for fan art. IÂ neededÂ fan art.

What I found, instead, was an ebook. Courtney Stevens, you rock.

THE BLUE-HAIRED BOY is Bodee’s story. It’s a prequel (can I call it that? It’s set before the Â events of FAKING NORMAL. It’s extra information and BOY IS IT LOVELY), but both books are stand alone. You read one, you’ll want to read the other.

I’m not sure which one I want you to read first. I would probably say, go with FAKING NORMAL and then read Bodee’s story, only because that way, you fall in love with Bodee in the order he was written. (Note that I have no idea if Courtney wrote TBHB first or second… but it’s listed second on her website, so I’m gonna stick with that order.) If you can’t stand sad books, then read TBHB first… and then you’ll be compelled to read FN, so whoops, sorry. Alexi and Bodee… they’re worth it.

I already wanted a boy like Bodee in my life, not even halfway through the story, but IÂ reallyÂ wanted one after the ebook. That’s a good story. Courtney has a way with words that I really appreciate and admire, and I spent most of the time reading both books wanting to hug the characters (and punch a couple. It’s not a high school book if you don’t want to punch someone).

The older I get, the weirder I find it to read books where the main characters are 16. I’ll continue doing so, don’t get me wrong, love me some YA novels… but I wasn’t like most of the characters in YA books. I didn’t drive, I didn’t really ride around with other 16 year olds that had cars, and I was home for dinner (almost) every night (shoutout to play practice). That said, the characters felt 16, but they didn’t feel childish. They didn’t feel fake. There were moments in FAKING NORMAL where I was like, yeah, this is what would happen in real life. (MINI SPOILER, but it’s on the back of the book… ) Bodee moves in with Alexi’s family. I won’t tell you why exactly, because that IS a spoiler, but it’s a logical move. Courtney sets it up so that it’s not a random thing – even less random if you read TBHB – and so I had no problem just going with it. Even after several days have gone by, it still seems like the very logical thing to happen.

I guess my point is, it’s not your typical high school book. Not even close. The problems they face are hard, and the emotions raw, and the situationÂ real.

(Please note that I’m an emotional sap, so just becauseÂ IÂ cried doesn’t mean you will. Also, I cry at both happy AND sad. So just go with it. This book makes you feel things, which for me also equals tears.)

The OPH we go to is in Park Ridge (aka Uptown… off of N Northwest Hwy between Ridge Terrace and Euclid Ave). Here’s the deal.
