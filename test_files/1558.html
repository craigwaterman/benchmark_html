Anxiety Can Kiss It

Temperature changes.
Surgeries and healing.
Packing and moving across the country.

What are----Things That Cause Fibro Flares----for 1000, Alex?

In just a matter of weeks, it was solidified that I was to move south to Charlotte, NC.  So I've been dealing with packing up my life (which is a long and tedious process I can't get through easily even though I'm in the smallest apartment in Ohio), finding a new apartment there, tying up loose ends here, finding the best way to relocate myself (movers vs UHaul), finding friends to help on either end of the move to load and unload my big pieces of furniture I will never be able to do myself, getting my dog's health squared away while we are still within range of the vet we know and like, getting my OWN health issues taken care of to include having had surgery on 7/8 which went very well, but is leaving me without much energy (as if I had much haha!)...the list is seemingly endless.  I have much more respect for my parents whom moved the family from Mexico City to South Carolina when I was 10 in just a week to be with my ailing grandmother.  How that happened so flawlessly, I will never understand!  Kudos to the parental unit for pulling it off!

The stress and (added) anxiety have made my flares become more frequent and stronger.  Dealing with the pain and trying to accomplish all of this is a very difficult task.  

I've noticed more and more over these last few months that people who are "normal" don't get that there is a difference between "worrying" and "stress" and TRUE "anxiety".  The type of anxiety that requires medical intervention just to deal without going into manic episodes on a frequent basis.  The world collapses around you and you can't break the glass bubble to get out of it-type of anxiety.  This only makes the Fibro pain increase and life stops.  

Stress and anxiety are evil.

Tuesday, June 9, 2015

Stress Organizing

When you get stressed out about...anything...your body as a Fibro patient tenses up and hurts more.  It's just the way we work.  The best way to deal with stress is to not have any.  HA!  In what perfect world, right?  

My coping mechanism for dealing with stress and anxiety lately has been reorganizing things.  Today, my desk suffered the wrath of my stress.  I spent about 4 hours taking everything out of my drawers, shelves, and desk and reorganizing the desk until I was happy with it.  Lots of new folders, lots of things were trashed, lots of coordination by size, color and type of items.  

Sounds like a good thing, right?  It's not.

Even though my desk is looking even better than it was, I'm hurting so much more than I already was.  They say exercise helps ease Fibro pain.  Thè thing is, moving hurts.  My legs are very swollen, my back is hurting, my shoulders and arms hurt more than normal, the backs of my thighs hurt regardless of how I sit, my hips are on fire, and shockingly, my scalp hurts as if I have indeed been scalped!  

I don't have a solution for dealing with stress.  Nor for not being in pain after doing any kind of activities.  My best advice is that after you do wind up in this position, plop down on a giant maternity pillow on top of your bed and rest while watching tv.  You've earned it.

Wednesday, May 27, 2015

A Great Fibro Makes the World Go Round

I just arrived at my rheumatologist appointment and am pretty happy to be here.  This doctor is amazing.  One of the best in the country.  It took me a long time to get in to see him for the first time since he is so great, his books were pretty packed.

I didn't like the rheumatologist I was seeing before.  I felt like she didn't spend time with me, didn't listen to what I was really saying, and just increased my medicine I was already taking and didn't like.  Her solution was always to give me cortisone shots and increase the dose of the one medication she had me on.  My primary care physician happened to know this world-renowned doctor I wanted to get in to see and couldn't, so he pulled out his BlackBerry (yep, that long ago), sent him a quick message saying he had a patient that couldn't get in to see him but really needed his help, and before we finished our conversation, we had a response from him saying I had an appointment in 2 weeks with him.  I started crying, as you could imagine.

The downside is that his schedule is packed.  The upside is that I can contact him via email and he responds very quickly, so I don't need to actually come in regularly.  This is the first time in about 18 months I'm coming in to see him in person. 

My medicines aren't responding like they used to since I'm having some absorption issues, so that's why I'm here in person today.  We are going to do a medicine overhaul and see what else we can try if not increase what I'm already on.  I'm struggling big time with sleeping, and the meds I am on used to help me sleep. 

So come on doc, Cilla needs some rest!

Saturday, May 23, 2015

Support Systems or Lack Thereof

I was speaking with a fellow Spoonie tonight about some of her current issues she's having with her Fibro.  We talked about a lot of different things all over the Fibro spectrum, but it lead us to the big topic I wanted to bring up tonight:  support systems.

Do you have one?  Who is it?  

I'm talking about someone you can go to and casually talk to about your Spissues (word for Spoonie-issues I just made up...spread it around.  Make it a thing.  I'll make an ecard for your convenience.) and  feel unjudged, not blown off, and genuinely heard?  If you have that person, or people, KUDOS to them!  Everyone needs those people.  

If you don't know someone in your circle who is also a Spoonie, the world can feel like a much bigger, lonelier place than it probably is.  We all need someone who can understand us.

By the way, because I got excited...




So, my friend was saying that her family doesn't support her like she would like.  I totally know the feeling.  You want to think that your family, out of anyone else, would be your #1 support system in this...right?

In my situation, I've tried giving examples of, "If this is difficult for you, imagine how it must be for me." I've been met with, "Everyone is going through something." and "Try not to take everything so serious." Bare in mind, none of those statements were in a harsh or malicious tone, just in a way that added anxiety and further deepened the depression that was already there.   

Is it wrong for a Spoonie to want that support from close family and friends?  I personally think not.  It's natural.  I also think it's natural for those people to not get it.  Unfortunately, that doesn't make our want for support any better nor our job to educate them (because we do have that responsibility if we want the support) any less complicated.  

Long ago I figured out that talking to people about my Fibromyalgia was off-putting.  So unless asked or truly necessary, I just kept the topic to myself.  Suffered in silence.  I still keep it that way, for the most part.  Outlets like this, my new Facebook group Fibromyalgia Fighters Support Group, the few friends I have who are Spoonies, and even just pinning on my Fibro boards on Pinterest help me not keep it bottled up inside.  

Anyone out there reading this who is currently bottling it up all to yourself.....STOP!  Comment on here to vent!  Join my FB group!  Find a local Fibro organization in your area.  Find a sounding board of some kind.  

xoxo,

Cilla

We Say Thank You, We Say Please & It's...Ways To Keep Busy When You're Sad

Yeah, I know the title makes no sense.  I may have been watching Orange Is The New Black today and got a little help from Crazy Eyes.  (PS....COME ON SEASON 3!)

 When you're a Spoonie, you have certain quirks.  Not all of us have the same ones, but a lot of us do.  Fibro fog is a very real problem for us.  It means you can go into a head space and forget things you KNOW.  I mean things like...your address or your spouses name.  When I worked in the corporate world, I'd always have a confidential go-to person I could say, "Hey, I'm having a moment, what's ________ again?"  That person I knew would help without laughing and knew why I was asking.

It was important because even though I was AMAZING at my job and usually one of the go-to people for others, I'd have these moments of Fibro Fog where I'd know it's happening, I'd know that I do know the information, but it's missing.  So if I tried to solve my work on my own, I could really mess up and I was just too much of a control freak for that.  If I went to just anyone, which I've done over the years, the reaction is usually met with some kind of mocking which just adds to the humiliation I already had for asking remedial questions.  People without FMS and whom don't experience Fibro Fog can't understand what it's like to need help with tasks you've accomplished on your own for a substantial number of years.  Fog can last from a few minutes to a few hours and even though you know it's happening, you can't get out of it. 

Funny thing is, with all the Fog, I one of my quirks is that I have the (AWFUL) ability to remember a lot of information I usually don't want to.  I truly could go without a calendar for the rest of my life and fine. Not to say I'm at a Rainman level, but for example...dates just stick with me.  I remember birth dates of kids from my 1st grade class.  True story.  (Not their names, but their birth dates lol!) I know, I know.  Many people do that, Cilla!

Let's throw in some of the 200+ co-existing symptoms of Fibromyalgia which we learned from Inspired Living With Fibromyalgia, shall we? 

Anxiety?  Check!
Bipolar?  Check!
Depression?  Check!

So what's the correlation and when are we going to get to the craft already?

May 18th, 2001, was my high school graduation!  A wonderful day!  I originally had the word "memory" in there, but I changed it because I actually don't remember anything about graduating.  I have several pictures and we look very happy, but I don't remember the memories.  I'm pretty sure it's nothing to do with being 14 years ago, as some may suggest.  I am more than certain the day been masked by other memories.

 May 18th, 2008, came and forever changed that memory for me.  A very, very difficult break-up I still mourn today complete with 6.5 years of memories, buying a house together, having to give up the house, and the ultimate betrayal:  cheating.  Which in hindsight, I suspect happened more than those last few months.  Probably the last half of the relationship, unfortunately.      

FMS makes it extremely difficult deal with traumatic events.  So today I decided to try to take my mind off what the day was as much as I possibly could.  (Because it's like having a little roll of film playing over and over in your head just for you, and that's just not fun unless it's starring Johnny Depp.) I tried to take a nap, but that lasted about an hour.  Then I decided to organize the kitchen pantry which did serve its purpose for a good while.  Then I got to the tea and chili sauce cabinet (don't ask) and decided...okay, it's time.

Since my head wasn't necessarily screwed on straight, I didn't take many pictures.  I thinks you'll get the idea of how I did this though.

Left Caddy: 80 (very loose) tea bags
Right Caddy: 120 (tightly packed) tea bags

I used:
1 brown lunch bag
2 sm brown shopping handled bags (you can omit above, my 2nd bag wasn't plain)
Scissors
Packing tape
Scotch tape
2 Containers of your choice, I used protein bar boxes

Steps:
-Gently open the paper bag into a rectangular shape.  I recommend opening from the bottom and cutting to avoid tearing.
-Tear off the handles and set aside.
-Cut the top part of your box leaving no lips behind.
-Use scotch tape to seal any openings around the perimeter of the box.  It does not need to be perfect.
-Wrap the box with the paper bag.  Unless you have extra paper, the inside of your container should be bare.
-Use packing tape to wrap the entire box, including the bottom.  I recommend starting from inside out, but this is not crucial.  
-Stack two of your handles together and cut the bottom and sides to ensure they match.
-Use packing tape to adhere to the inside of the caddy.

What's A "Spoonie", Anyway?

Chances are, if you found this blog, you Googled the term "Fibromyalgia" or "Chronic Fatigue Syndrome"...am I right?  Maybe you know someone with it and want to learn more or you have it yourself and are just trying to cope.  However you landed here, I'm glad you did.  I decided to start this blog to share my story as someone who's lived over half their existence with this life-altering diagnosis.

If you have been diagnosed with Fibromyalgia and/or CFS, I'm sorry.  I truly am.  There's no cure for what we have as of yet.  Doctors don't know what causes it and that should probably be step 1.  I've heard over recent years that severe traumas "can cause" FMS, though, I certainly did not fall into that category.  I was diagnosed at age 15 after a long, bumpy, 2-year process of trying to find a correct diagnosis that was not "you're crazy" and "you're seeking attention".  I did get many, many incorrect, bandaid diagnoses along the way like rheumatoid arthritis (which is actually not too far fetched, but still wrong), plantar fasciitis, carpal tunnel, regular arthritis, I was put in those moon walking boots as well as full plaster casts on my arms while I had no broken bones, given crutches, etc.  Oh, the fun of becoming a Spoonie!  My family tree has a large number of Spoonies all on the maternal side as well, but I'm the youngest.

So what's a "Spoonie", anyway?

The term "Spoonie" comes from The Spoon Theory written by Christine Miserandino.

A brief summary of the theory is that Christine and her best friend were at lunch when Christine was asked what it's like to be sick.  Christine actually has Lupus, not FMS.  The two are related.  Many who have one have the other, sadly.  Both are auto-immune disorders, which is why the term applies to both of us.  Christine assumed her best friend just knew what it was like and she struggled to explain to her what it's like to live with a chronic, invisible illness.  At that point, she picked up all the spoons from her table and from the next table and handed them to her friend and said, "Okay, now YOU have Lupus."  After handing the spoons to her friend, she told her the spoons basically allow you to do what you need to during the day, like energy.  As she named off her To Do list, Christine took one of her twelve spoons away.  Her friend quickly realized she had to budget her energy wisely or risk running out of spoons.  Standing up from the bed, putting clothes on, washing your face, making breakfast, etc.  All worth individual spoons and could deplete your budget before lunch.

If you have just 5 free minutes, I URGE you to pop over to her website linked above and read it.  You can even download the PDF from her page to read later.  You'll be very glad you did.

So from then on, "The Spoon Theory" was born.  Those of us with illnesses others can't see on the outside, but haunt us on the inside have since been referred to as Spoonies.  I'm not sure I could have come up with a better explanation for the fatigue and pain we endure, could you?

Thank you, Christine! 

 I'm just getting started with my blog.  I plan on writing fairly often. It won't be directly about FMS or CFS every time.  My life is much more than that, but it is impacted by it in just about every way.  I will be on posting about all sorts of things including my weight loss journey and because I'm itching to do it, a makeup tutorial or two.

These are all connected to life as a Spoonie!  But how?  Stay tuned to find out!
