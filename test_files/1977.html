The people have spoken: America’s favorite way to save energy during summer is…

  • By Barry Fischer and Nate Kaufman
  • July 25, 2013

Summer is legendary for high energy usage. June, July, and August unleash by far America’s most prodigious electricity consumption of any consecutive 3-month period.

Likewise, Opower’s own energy data storehouse – the world’s largest, spanning more than 50 million households – suggests that this year’s summer energy consumption is off to a predictably running start. Not to mention that just last Friday, New York City set an all-time record for its electricity demand. Such is life in a country where nearly 90% of homes have air conditioners, up from 63% just 20 years ago.

But the hottest part of the year needn’t be an electron-gulping free-for-all. There are plenty of things you can do to stay cool while ensuring that your electric bill doesn’t spiral out of control. Querying Google for the phrase “summer energy saving tips” serves up no less than 142,000 search results. Our mission today is to add our own list of summer tips to the mix. Well, to be fair, it’s not our list of tips. It’s yours…

We’ve examined an anonymous, geographically diverse sample of energy efficiency plans from more than 220,000 utility customers — based on their visits to and engagement with Opower’s energy information web portal, now available to customers of 75 utility companies in 30+ US states (and several other countries across the world).

Based on an aggregate pool of 2.77 million clicks that utility customers logged in order to construct their own energy savings plans (via clickable button options like the ones shown above), we were able to calculate a popularity index for each summer tip (see Methodology).

The result: a quantitative ranking of America’s most popular ways to save energy in summer.

Did your favorite summer energy saving tip make the cut?

             1. Set your thermostat wisely

When you get home from a long day at work and a hot commute, your instinct may be to crank up the AC and turn your house into an ice palace. But making small changes to the way you use your thermostat can go a very long way in saving energy: each degree of adjustment has an estimated 3-5% impact on your monthly cooling costs.

In our sample of American households, the thermostat is the place to go for summer energy savings. The number of households who report maintaining wise thermostat settings outnumber those who don’t by a ratio of 22-to-1.

The U.S. Department of Energy suggests setting your summertime thermostat to as high a temperature as you’re comfortable with: as a default, they recommend 78°F when you’re home and awake, and bumping it up further when you’re away from home or sleeping.

And remember, when you first turn on your AC, there’s no point in turning it to a low temperature, idealistically believing it will go into turbo-freeze mode. In reality, calling for a lower temperature than where you want your home to end up won’t speed up the cooling process. Keep it simple and stick to 78°F.

A programmable/automated wi-fi thermostat can help make managing your home’s temperature a breeze. In our data sample, nearly 40,000 customers report using one. Speaking of breeze, don’t forget to generate some refreshing wind chill from your fans. It’ll allow you to raise your thermostat setting about 4°F and stay just as comfortable.

 2. Let your air conditioner breathe

Have you ever tried breathing with a sock over your mouth? Neither have we. There’s no sense in making it harder for air to get to and from your lungs. Similarly, it doesn’t make sense to obstruct the air flowing into and out of your air conditioning system. Nearly 100,000 households in our sample have taken this basic and easy-to-address concept to heart.

You too can get cooler more quickly and save around $20 per year on your utility bill by taking a few minutes to: 1) remove weeds, leaves, and other debris near your AC’s outdoor unit, and 2) clear away objects that may be blocking indoor AC vents (offenders may include a chair, rug, or beanbag pillow).

 3. Block out the sun

Here comes the sun, little darling. But that doesn’t mean you should let it into your house. Take it from the more than 100,000 households in our sample who are drawing their blinds, using shutters and awnings, and even planting “shade trees” in their yard: the popular and energy-efficient thing to do in summer is to give sunshine the cold shoulder.

Blocking sunlight will help prevent your home from warming up in the first place, so there’s less need to run the AC. Light-colored blinds and draperies can reduce solar heat gain in a home by 33-45%, and external window awnings can reduce it by up to 77%.

And according to field studies conducted in Sacramento, shade trees can reduce cooling costs by 30% and overall summertime electricity costs by more than 5%.

 4. Seal up your cooling system and maximize efficiency

Many of the simplest and most effective ways to save energy hinge on small changes to human behavior around the house — like adjusting the thermostat, clearing around vents, and closing blinds. But it’s also a good idea to make sure that your house itself is on its best summer behavior.

Is hot air sneaking into your home — or cold air escaping — through doors, walls, or a poorly insulated attic?  Are there tiny holes or cracks in your ducts that cause your air conditioner to work harder? Is your AC system itself less efficient than it could be (a Seasonal Energy Efficiency Ratio of 13 is now the industry standard)?

Tens of thousands of homes in our dataset indicate they’re complementing their behavioral summer adjustments with building-focused maintenance and efficiency upgrades throughout their homes.

 5. Trim hot afternoon electricity usage (and get paid for it)

More and more, utilities are encouraging their customers to reduce electricity use during the hottest hours of summer, in order to prevent rolling blackouts and avoid exorbitant costs from using backup power plants.

Programs to address residential peak demand have been waxing in size and importance in recent years. There are now more than 300 peak load reduction programs that spring into action during the summer, reaching more than 5 million US households.

In the western region, where one of our largest utility partners has a well-developed program to reward peak reduction efforts, tens of thousands of customers report that one of their favorite ways to save energy in summer is by making small cutbacks to AC usage on hot days. It doesn’t hurt that they earn a $50 annual bonus from their power company for doing so.  As more utilities deploy innovative peak demand management approaches – like offering dynamic cash incentives and providing real-time insights to customers — we expect these strategies to become even more popular in the coming the years.

What are the best ways to save during peak times? Air conditioning is always a good bet: turn up the AC temperature a few degrees and use fans to stay cool. Also, try to delay using appliances and electronics until later in the evening to help alleviate the afternoon stress on the electricity grid.

 6. Maintain your pool efficiently

Most of us don’t have swimming pools, but those who do (5.1 million American yards have in-ground pools as of 2013) are probably aware of the colossal amount of energy it takes to pump and heat the water that fills them. Our statistical analysis on this blog last year found that homes with pools use 49% more electricity per year than non-pool homes. Perhaps that’s not surprising when you consider that a pool’s motorized filtration pump circulates more water each day than a human drinks in a lifetime.

But there are some good strategies to keep pool energy use down, including installing energy-efficient pumps and heaters, covering your pool to reduce the need to heat it, decreasing the run time of your pump, and lowering the temperature of the water. Based on customer activity on Opower’s web portal in states that have a high penetration of pools (e.g. Arizona, Florida, and New York), pool-related efficiency adjustments consistently rank among the five most popular summer energy saving tips.

There you have it. America’s favorite ways to save energy in summer. Hungry for more summer efficiency advice? Or ready to start preparing for low utility bills in autumn, winter, and spring? You can check out a comprehensive list of energy saving advice on your utility’s website or the new Energy Saver portal from the US Department of Energy.

Methodology:

Data analysis is based on an anonymous, geographically diverse sample of energy saving plans from 222,607 unique utility customers — constructed via their visits to and engagement with Opower’s energy information web portal. Tip popularity is inferred from an aggregate pool of 2.77 million clicks that convey users’ preferences on how to save energy.

To determine popularity rankings of each tip category, we computed adoption-to-rejection ratios, defined as: (# of customers who report adopting a given tip) / (# of customers who report not being interested in a given tip). Adoption is defined as the sum of unique customers who report having done or planning to pursue a given tip. Rejection is defined as the number of customers clicking “No Thanks” in response to a given tip.

Adoption-to-rejection ratios for each category of savings actions were as follows: Set Thermostat Wisely (22), Let AC Breathe (18), Keep out Sun (12), Structural Improvements (6), Peak Savings (3). Given that relatively few homes own pools but they are responsible for a disproportionate fraction of home energy use, the pool savings category was included based on the authors’ judgment, rather than for its specific adoption-to-rejection ratio.

Data Privacy: All data analyzed here are anonymous and treated in strict adherence to Opower’s Data Principles.

About Outlier

Outlier explores trends in how people are using energy in the US and around the world. Pulling from an unprecedented (and still growing) amount of energy data—currently drawn from 50 million homes—Opower crunches energy-use information from more than 90 utility partners every day, and cross-references that with weather, household, and demographic information to produce compelling analyses in the Outlier series.
