From Start to Finish – Making a Budget

January 13, 2014 By Amy

Today we’re going to tackle our very first topic in the From Start to Finish series – making a budget.

For all of the negativity associated with it, you would think “budget” was a dirty word.  Most of us sigh at the thought of taking the time to manage our money because we think it will mean fewer Starbucks stops and more commitment. While it is true that creating a budget takes time on the front end, the rewards are worth the energy that it takes to set up a workable plan.

Even if you have never sat down to make a budget, you probably already have a rough draft of one in your head.  You know that each month, you make a certain amount of money, and that from that money you must 1) pay your bills, 2) pay for food, 3) live.  Figuring out how to do those three things with the money that you have is all that making a budget really is.  In order to get from the rough draft in your head to a solid budget on paper, we’ll go through three different things – why keep a budget, what the components of a budget are, and what to do with money you have left over.

Let’s get started!

Why keep a budget?

Ok, that’s kind of like asking “why run?” or “why enjoy good food?” The answers are endless and vary from person to person.  But the simplest answer that I could think of is this –

It’s important to keep a budget so that you get the things you want without sacrificing the things you need.  

The idea that a budget is something there to restrict you couldn’t be farther from the truth.  You budget so that you can buy the pair of black booties that you’ve had your eye on for years, while making sure that you do it at a time when you won’t be sacrificing the rent to get them.

A budget keeps this equation in mind: Income – (Expenses + Savings) = What’s Left

Getting started

1. Determine your monthly expenses and itemize them.  Add up the cost of rent, water, gas/electric, gasoline, etc…Do not include incidental expenses (so if you had to get a new tire last month, don’t include that in this number). Also, just because you feel like you NEED those black booties, they aren’t considered a primary monthly expense, so they don’t go in this category.

2. Figure out how much you spend each month on food.  This is a doozy and could have its own blog post.  Start with the amount of money that you spend on groceries.  If you’re not sure, look at your bank statement and take the average of what you spent over the last three months.  Then, determine how much money you spent last month eating out.

3. Subtract monthly expenses (including food) from your monthly income and see how much you have left.  Ideally, you’d have at least 1/4 of your income left.  This is important because from this money you will take savings and anything else that you choose to purchase during the month.

4. Decide how much of your income you want to save, and subtract that money from your balance Saving 10 percent of your income is a good place to start.

5. Once you’ve taken care of bills, food, and saving, booties, here you come!

But wait…what if I don’t have enough left over?

Not having enough money is something that most of us struggle with.  It’s easy to adjust “up” to fit an increased income, but not so easy to adjust “down.”  That means even when you make more money, chances are you will also spend more money.  At the same time, if you’re just getting used to living on your own, you will probably have to adjust your purchases to reflect a smaller income now that your parents aren’t supporting you.  This may also be the case if you have started a new job or venture that leaves you with a lower or uncertain income.

One of the main points of making a budget is to see where you are spending your money and determine whether or not you are happy with that allocation.  If you spent 1/3 of your income on eating out, but you also feel that you don’t have enough money left over for other things, it may be time to think about being more strategic at the grocery store so that you can eat out less.  That doesn’t mean you can never go out to eat again, (I believe going out to a nice restaurant is one of life’s gifts!), but it does mean that going out to a restaurant might be a once a week treat instead of something you do multiple times per week.  Take some of the money you were spending on going out to eat, and put it away so that in a couple of months, those booties will be yours.

Once you have a good look at your budget, you might decide that you want to take the money you have left over and allocate it to other things, like saving for a trip or the holidays.  However, DON’T completely eliminate your fun money fund, even if it only consists of a few dollars a month.  You never know when you might see the perfect item for your house, when you will HAVE to buy that top for yourself (or for a child/cousin/mother/dog), or when you might just need a pick-me-up and want to treat yourself.  If you set this money aside, you won’t find yourself taking money away from other things in order to buy those treats.  Because, let’s not kid ourselves, you will buy them.

The first step to creating a budget is sitting down and doing it.  I’ll go into more detail about easy ways to keep track of your spending as well as how to save money as the From Start to Finish series continues next week.

Now I want to hear from you! Do you keep a budget? What is the hardest thing about doing so? What advice do you have?
