How to Cultivate a Positive, Healthy Mindset About Food

One way to cultivate a positive, healthy mindset about food is by paying attention to our mindset in relation to food and what role it has in your life. A mindset that is focused on the nutritional value of the food consumed and it’s role as fuel for your body’s optimal physical health and functioning.

Understanding what your current mindset is will help you decide what, if any, adjustments need to be made to support a healthy relationship with food versus one that supports disordered eating behaviors.  The mindset you adopt plays a huge role in the success or failure of any long term changes you make in your eating habits and your perception of yourself.

First, let’s consider a couple of different mindsets and whether they are positive or negative:

  1. A “skinny” mindset is one that is often influenced by the media “ideal” of what beautiful and successful people look like.  This mindset can be driven by the need for perfectionism and is often characterized by rigid, “black or white” thinking.  An example of this type of thinking is categorizing all food as either “good or bad.” Restrictive eating or anorexic behaviors often stem from this type of mindset. Guilt over not following self-imposed restrictions or failing to meet self-imposed standards of “perfection” may lead someone with this mindset to engage in bulimic behaviors. (negative outcome)
  2. A “helpless” mindset is one in which you lack feelings of self worth and feel hopelessly dis-empowered. It’s often characterized by an external locus of control, feeling as if outside forces control your life and that there’s no point in trying to change. An example of this is blaming your boss for your misery at work and then eating a pint of ice cream to feel better about a bad day at work even though you told yourself that you would start eating healthier. Someone who is an Emotional eater or a Binge eater may have this mindset.  (negative outcome)
  3. A “healthy” mindset is one in which you feel empowered to make major decisions in your life and is focused on living a healthy lifestyle.  It’s characterized by practicing the Art of Self Love and emphasizes your strengths, talents, and abilities.  It’s self nurturing and self-empowering.  A healthy mindset is supported by an internal locus of control.  There is a sense of control over your life and how it will turn out. An example of this type of mindset is choosing to go to the gym after a tough work day spent dealing with a boss who is demanding and unappreciative.  After working out your frustrations, you make a decision to start looking for other job opportunities. (positive outcome)

These mindsets are not all-inclusive and you may have one that is a hybrid or one that you would characterize differently.* The list’s purpose is to get you thinking about your mindset and whether or not it supports your goals for a healthy life.

Strategies for Cultivating a Positive, Healthy Mindset:

  1.  Eat foods that make you feel good when you eat them AND long afterwards.
  • Nourishing foods that taste good. (I love making my own granola that is crunchy and loaded with cinnamon flavor to top off Greek Yogurt.)
  • Foods that may not be as high in nutritional value but that add significant pleasure to your life experience. (For me, that would definitely be rich, spicy Mexican food and creamy milk chocolate that melts in my mouth!)
  • Questions to ask yourself before eating or drinking anything:
    • Does this food support my desire to nourish my body for optimal health and fitness?
      • Consider it a green light if you answer yes.

    • If it doesn’t, will I feel ashamed and/or guilty for eating it later?
      • Consider it a red light if you answer yes.
    • Even if the food doesn’t support my nutritional goals, will eating it enrich my life experience?
      • Another green light if you answer yes.

 

 

The key to eating foods that have little or no nutritional value is to do so without guilt and in moderation.  I don’t “diet” or tell myself that a specific food is off limits.  Instead, I incorporate all foods (okay, not liver or squid or brussel sprouts or lima beans ) into my overall food intake for the day in appropriate proportions according to their nutritional and caloric value.  I don’t count calories specifically, I just make adjustments when I know I’m going to be eating a “heavier” meal than usual or chose to have a high calorie treat.

One of my adjustments related to moderation that doesn’t leave me feeling deprived is to have a “snack size” peanut butter snickers rather than consuming a full size candy bar. My husband (who has no food issues other than not eating the last bite of crust on his sandwiches) teases me when he watches me savor a snack size snickers. I make it last for four or more bites, chewing it slowly so I can thoroughly enjoy the difference flavors and textures while inhaling the scrumptious chocolate, peanut buttery aroma.  Instead of letting his teasing embarrass or anger me, I use it for motivation to enhance the experience to theatrical proportions!

 

2.  Practice Intuitive Eating

  • Determine if your hunger is physical or emotional
    • Did your hunger come on suddenly or was the onset gradual?
      • Sudden onset is a sign that the hunger is based in emotions
      • Gradual onset is a sign that the hunger is physical
    • What are you feeling?
    • Do you stop eating when you feel satiated?

3.  Savor Every Bite, Not Just The First One

  • Chew slowly
  • Enjoy the texture and taste of every bite
  • Put your attention on your food, not the television, computer, or a book
  • When you’ve become accomplished and confident at eating mindfully…Share your eating experience with others – it’ll enhance your mood & the food will taste better as long as you don’t lose sight of your healthy intentions and shift your focus from eating mindfully to eating mindlessly!

4.  Practice, Practice, Practice.

  • Healthy and mindful eating is a habit…just like the disordered eating pattern you may have developed.  Whatever behavior is consistently practiced and reinforced will become more ingrained and more resistant to change – make your eating habit a healthy one!

 

Develop a healthier relationship with food and healthy fitness habits through practice and by increasing your eating awareness and your ability to focus on the present moment.

 

*If your mindset differs from those listed above, I’d loved to hear the details.  I may even add it to the list to benefit others who are struggling with their food relationship.
