Money View: 8 Money Mindsets that Rule your Finances

Scott MartinAugust 10, 2015August 21, 2015

One of the most valuable concepts I've used to improve my personal finances comes from a
CD called Money View by New York Times bestselling author Chris Brady. In it he explains that how you think about money, or your attitude towards money, ultimately decides whether money plays a positive or negative role in your life.

Learning which money mindsets I had, which ones would better serve me and taking the time to think about my thinking, improved my results dramatically.

Which money views represent where you are today, and where would you like to go?

1. Money as a Mystery

Money is a mystery to people who:

  • Don't understand why money comes in or have a plan for how to utilize money to serve their wants, needs, purpose and vision.

  • Think wealth is based on luck, their birth, circumstances or anything other than the solid application of wealth principles.

  • Often find themselves thinking about fairness and equality in economic outcomes, and believe that the government can and should promote either of these.

  • Define the word "afford" by what they can finance through debt.

  • Finance the purchase of assets that depreciate, or lessen in value over time.

  • Haven't learned the difference between an investment and an expense.

For instance, a person that sees money as a mystery would never buy the Money View CD for $10. They would see it as a needless expense, rather than a valuable investment in their thinking. Josee and I have probably listened to this CD a dozen times between us, and it's improved our finances dramatically.

As Chris Brady says:

A thing is worth what it can do for you, not what you can pay for it.

The way out of this money view is to, at a minimum, learn the basics of the offense, defense and playing field of finances.

2. Money as a Master

This is another mindset that the majority of Americans have experienced in the past or are experiencing right now. I've lived here for much of my life. In this state of mind, lack of money dominated my thinking, and created much stress. Money may be a master for those who:

  • Exchange time for money, and only receive money when they are working.

  • Don't have a budget or a plan for directing their finances.

  • Have trouble living within their means or believe that they "deserve" material items that they haven't saved the cash to pay for.

  • Fail to invest in their future through savings and personal development.

  • Don't understand the crippling effects of compound interest.

Alternatively, begin to master money by following a sound financial plan. In addition, develop some source of pipeline income that will pay you in the future, repeatedly, for the efforts you put in today. Excellent options include starting your own online business or enrolling in a network marketing business, both of which can be done while maintaining your "9 to 5" job that pays the bills.

These actions have allowed me to be a better master over my money, and can do the same for you!

3. Money as a Monster

When money is a monster, unfortunately it tends to be a scarce monster! But it's super abundant in your thoughts. Here again, lack of money dominates your thinking, even to the point where it's the first thing you think about when you wake up. Money may be a monster for those who: 

  • Are hounded by debt collectors. 

  • Are facing foreclosure or repossession.

  • Often fight with their spouse over how much either are spending on things the other deems frivolous.

Money is now a monster, and people are here because they lived #2 (Money as a Master) for too long, without acting on a solid plan to get the heck out of there!

4. Money as a Means

Whew! I need a break from the negative for a minute. Money as a means is one of the healthy ways of looking at money. Money is a means for those who:

  • Realize that money is value neutral, neither good nor bad.

  • Understand that it is not money that is the root of all evil, but the love or worship of money.

  • Know that money is a tool that can help them live a happier and more abundant life, as long as it's kept in the proper perspective.

  • Own their money, their money doesn't own them!

Money is simply a means.

5. Money as a Monument

When using money as a means goes in a negative direction, money can become a monument to glorify self. This mindset includes:

  • Use of credit to "keep up with the Joneses" or to appear to have a lifestyle above what is true.

  • The desire to purchase status in the eyes of others.

If I buy a flashy, expensive car (that I can afford, in cash), because I like the way it looks and love to drive it, then I am using money as a means to fulfill a desire. Some of this is healthy in life. If I buy the same car, but do so because I want others to think I'm cool, or wealthy, or better than they are, then I'm on a destructive path. It's all in the mindset!

Money as a monument usually is a spiritual problem as well. To paraphrase Chris Brady, your life is meant to be a monument to your God. Not to yourself.

6. Money as a Motivator

Just as money is a means, money can also be an excellent motivator. This is also value neutral, as it fully depends on the goodness of the holder's intentions. As Chris Brady says in the CD:

(Money as a motivator is) a dangerous or productive weapon, depending on the heart of the person that wields it.

An example of using money as a motivator in a positive way is to set a goal that you wish to accomplish that will require you to serve lots of people, and plan a reward you will buy yourself when you reach your goal that will inspire you to do your personal best. If you find this works for you, then money can probably motivate you to greater service than you're currently giving.

7. Money as a Minimizer

In many American communities, there are more people doing "well enough" without having to expend a lot of effort than there are people living in poverty. And in many ways the problem can be worse than poverty, because the person who uses money as a minimizer usually doesn't realize there is a problem.

"I'm doing pretty good.." is the mantra of the minimizer. He is comfortable, and his comfort has led to laziness. He ceases to pursue a purpose, or perhaps he never had one, and because he has nothing to strive for, he makes a passion out of his entertainment or hobbies. He needs to wake up, get off his couch, turn off the tv and find people he can serve, a passion he can pursue, and a purpose he can give his life to!

If I sound harsh on this one, that's because I'm talking to myself! When I've gone through unproductive periods, and I've gone through plenty of them, this is usually the problem.

The solution: Find a dream that will drive you! What is your passion? Follow it. What aspect of society bothers you most? Get out there and make it better! Serve someone!

8. Money as a Maximizer

This is the mindset we should all strive for!

When money is a maximizer, you are driven to use your gifts and talents to acquire money that you will then leverage in your pursuit of a higher calling. What you are maximizing here is not your own pleasure or your own glory, but rather your contribution to the world around you. You can always improve how you impact the world by leveraging money more effectively.

You've been called to make an impact in the world, starting right where you're at right now. By learning which money mindsets to avoid and embracing the healthy ones, money will be a force for good in your life, and never a negative. You can do it!
