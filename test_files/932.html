Monday, October 6, 2008

Prescription Drug Abuse on the Rise

The need for concern: misuse and abuse of prescription medication is on the rise in this country, and its debilitating effects are NOT exclusive.

Unintentional poisoning deaths involving narcotics and hallucinogens grew 55 percent from 1999 to 2004. Research suggests this is an increase attributed primarily to prescription painkillers. (CDC, 2007)

Unintentional poisoning deaths involving psychotherapeutic drugs, such as sedative-hypnotics and anti-depressants, grew 84 percent from 1999 to 2004. (CDC, 2007)

Between the years of 1995 and 2005, treatment admissions for abuse of prescription pain relievers grew more than 300 percent. (TEDS, 2007)

Though most people take prescription medications responsibly, an estimated 48 million people (ages 12 and older) have used prescription drugs for nonmedical reasons in their lifetimes. This represents about 20 percent of the U.S. population. (NIDA, 2008)

The elderly are among those most vulnerable to prescription drug abuse or misuse because they are prescribed more medications than their younger counterparts.

Sunday, September 21, 2008

High at School


Is your teen abusing prescription drugs?


Vicodin abuse, in the past year, has been particularly high among high school students, with nearly one in 10 seniors reporting taking it in the past year without a doctor’s approval. (MTF, 2007)

As of 2008, teens admit prescription drugs as being the second most easily accessible to them, the first being marijuana; they view the drugs as ‘safe’ highs because a doctor prescribed them. (SD, 2008)

In addition to the increasing number of medicines being prescribed for a variety of health problems, some medications can easily get into the wrong hands from online pharmacies. Most of these are legitimate businesses that provide an important service; however, some online pharmacies dispense medications without a prescription and without requesting proof of identification; this allows minors to order the medications easily over the internet.

Alarming Statistics: The 2004 National Institute on Drug Abuse’s Monitoring the Future survey of 8th, 10th, and 12th graders found that 9 percent of seniors reported using Vicodin without a prescription in the past year, and 5 percent reported using OxyContin- making these medications among the most commonly abused prescription drugs by adolescents.(NIDA, 2008)

Prescription Drug Abuse and the Elderly

The over-prescribing of medications is a problem mostly found within the elderly population. (NIDA, 2008) Although there are plausible reasons for taking numerous medications at once, such as disease, or the loss of a spouse, this allows an older adult to more easily become addicted to prescribed medications. According to USA Today, the group of drugs that is most abused by elderly patients are called benzodiazepines, which include sleep aids and tranquilizers. These are often prescribed to senior citizens during a time of stress and trauma, as they work to calm a person down by slowing down their brain activity. (USA, 2001) Also, in the elderly population, physicians commonly approve refills because of the nature of the situation for which the medication is prescribed. This can lead to an addiction to these drugs, and ultimately prescription drug abuse.

To help prevent elderly abuse of prescription drugs it is a good idea to monitor them for signs that they may be taking medications for reasons other than which they were prescribed. Also, involvement of an outside party, such as the children of the elderly adult, can prevent the unnecessary continuation of prescription medication, thus preventing unintentional abuse.

The Proper Way to Dispose of Prescription Drugs


To prevent others from abusing the prescription drugs you have, be sure to properly dispose of your unneeded or expired drugs. To properly dispose of your old medications take them out of the original container and place the pills into something that might be undesirable, like cat litter, grease, or something else to that effect, and then throw them away. Do not flush medications down the toilet unless the label says it is safe to do so. Below is a list of FDA approved drugs that are able to be flushed:

Actiq (fentanyl citrate)
Daytrana Transdermal Patch (methylphenidate)
Duragesic Transdermal System (fentanyl)
OxyContin Tablets (oxycodone)
Avinza Capsules (morphine sulfate)
Baraclude Tablets (entecavir)
Reyataz Capsules (atazanavir sulfate)
Tequin Tablets (gatifloxacin)
Zerit for Oral Solution (stavudine)
Meperidine HCl Tablets
Percocet (Oxycodone and Acetaminophen)
Xyrem (Sodium Oxybate)
Fentora (fentanyl buccal tablet)

Disposing of old prescriptions properly is an important step to preventing drug abuse.
