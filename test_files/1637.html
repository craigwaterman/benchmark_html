Johnson: Writing style

Eschew thesaurophilia

Dec 4th 2015, 14:30 by R.L.G.

  • Add this article to your reading list by clicking this button

  • Tweet

IT’S rare for a bit of writing advice to get something quite this wrong. The headline from the Wall Street Journal says it all:

‘Use More Expressive Words!’ Teachers Bark, Beseech, Implore. To encourage lively writing, instructors put certain words to rest; no more ‘fun’

Though it purports to be a trend piece, it is an old practice: teachers not just encouraging but requiring students to vary their vocabulary by banning certain common, plain English words, and making the students to choose from a list of bigger and fancier words to replace them.

What’s wrong with making students vary their vocabulary? Nothing, in principle. But there are practical reasons why this is a terrible bit of pedagogy.

Common words are common for a reason; they are useful. The teacher described in the story who bans ““Good, bad, nice, a lot, OK, fun, thing and stuff” in her students’ writing has her heart in the right place, but Johnson is willing to bet that if she writes on anything like a regular basis herself, she can’t get through a day without one of these words.  Apparently another teacher in the school has gone further, banning the likes of “go” and “see”.

This is madness; it’s all very well to “amble” or “strut” or “mosey” somewhere, if that is exactly the right word to use—but sometimes a writer doesn’t want to call attention to the manner of going. Replacing “see” with the likes of “observe" or “spy" or “perceive” or "glimpse" grabs attention that the writer might want to focus elsewhere:  “Steve was walking down Main Street when suddenly he saw a man in a bright-red lobster suit” is not improved by turning it into “Steve was ambling down Main Street when suddenly he perceived a man in a bright-red lobster suit.” “Amble” and “perceive” hold the reader’s focus too long, detracting from the punch delivered at the end.

This is one reason why great writers often give the opposite advice. Hemingway was a master of knowing how to make the fewest and simplest words tell. Churchill, who knew how to balance and measure out a sentence, said “short words are best, and old words, when short, are best of all.” (The Economist’s style-book editor once took this so close to his heart that he wrote a leader with only one-syllable words.) Orwell said “never use a long word where a short one will do.”  Elmore Leonard, a novelist famous for his gritty and realistic style, made one of his ten rules of writing “Never use a verb other than ‘said’ to carry dialogue.” (His summary of the ten rules: “If it sounds like writing, I rewrite it.”)

Not everyone has to write like Churchill, Orwell, Hemingway, Leonard or The Economist, of course. (And “never” is best avoided in this kind of rule.) But even writers who spin out long sentences know just how to do it. William Faulkner, often considered Hemingway’s stylistic foil, didn’t mindlessly vary his vocabulary. Take this sentence from “The Bear”:

But this time it was no deer, no ringing chorus of dogs running strong on a free scent, but a moiling yapping an octave too high, with something more than indecision and even abjectness in it, not even moving very fast, taking a long time to pass completely out of hearing, leaving then somewhere in the air that echo, thin, slightly hysterical, abject, almost grieving, with no sense of a fleeing, unseen, smoke-colored, grass-eating shape ahead of it, and Sam, who had taught him first of all to cock the gun and take position where he could see everywhere and then never move again, had himself moved up beside him; he could hear Sam breathing at his shoulder, and he could see the arched curve of the old man’s inhaling nostrils. 

Despite its length, it is easy to follow. And despite his reputation for fancy words, just one, “moiling”, sticks out here (meaning working hard). Nothing else slows the reader down. “Moiling” shines like a gem in an elegant but simple setting.

To become a better runner requires little more than running a lot. But to become a better writer is not the same: mindless, endless writing will not do. Instead, becoming a better writer requires reading—lots and lots of it. From Leonard to Faulkner, a young writer should read as much as possible in as many different styles. An individual voice develops out of that contact; an intermediate writer may learn by aping a favourite author, and a mature one will synthesise the best reading experiences with a confident voice of their own.

Learning to write by endless reading has the benefit of a being a lot more fun than mindlessly looking things up in a thesaurus. It is also a lot less moil.

I think that this article underscores that while technically English is a big fat language with a big fat thesaurus that spans from Australia to Zimbabwe, in practice the Australian English (even if we break it down into spoken English, official English and literary English) is quite different from the Zimbabwe version. Accrux Dec 5th 2015 2:42 GMT

Some people have a Thesaurus at hand whenever they write and obviously keep using it. The main reason for it is to impress those who read what they write, to look knowledgeable, but they only show their snobbishness and how pedantic (in the sense of "a person who adheres rigidly to book knowledge without regard to common sense") they are. It's typical of the nouveaux riches (social, intellectual or both). What I find remarkable is that they don't even seem to realise how easily detectable they are, how ridiculous and pathetic they look to others.

Personally I am fond of plain English (and plain Spanish, plain French, etc) which is not necessarily incompatible with a technical vocabulary (naval, astronomical, etc), with some lingoes and jargons or with Greek, Latin or German words, terms and phrases. as long as one uses them when it's really necessary to express or describe something. For example, I use the German word 'weltanschauung' because it's the best way I know of expressing what I want to say; I am aware that quite a few people will not understand it and will not have a dictionary at hand, but now, with the Internet and Google that isn't a problem any longer. I see a language as a tool to communicate, as a a means to an end rather than an end in its own right. Baroque, florid and involved writing bore me, tire me and often irritate me.

Quite a few Anglo-American people are, alas, impressed by Greek and Latin, and many Anglo-Saxon/Germanic English words have a latinate equivalent, for example freedom and liberty, but also aware and cognizant, start and commence, fatherly and paternal, rest and repose, loathe and detest &c. The idea of the people I referred to above is to use a Thesaurus to get those equivalent words which they don't usually remember by heart, use them instead of the 'normal', common English words... and look 'interesting' and knowledgeable. A pathetic pose.

It Was a Dark and Stormy Night

A novel by SNOOPY

PART 1

It was a dark and stormy night. Suddenly, a shot rang out! A door slammed. The maid screamed.
Suddenly, a pirate ship appeared on the horizon! While millions of people were starving, the king lived in luxury. Meanwhile, on a small farm in Kansas, a boy was growing up.

PART 2

A light snow was falling, and the little girl in the tattered shawl had not sold a violet all day.
At that very moment, a young intern at City Hospital was making an important discovery. The mysterious patient in Room 213 had finally awakened. She moaned softly. Could it be that she was the sister of the boy in Kansas who loved the girl with the tattered shawl who was the daughter of the maid who had escaped from the pirates? The intern frowned.
"Stampede!" the foreman shouted, and forty thousand head of cattle thundered down on the tiny camp. The two men rolled on the ground grappling beneath the murderous hooves. A left and a right. A left. Another left and right. An uppercut to the jaw. The fight was over. And so the ranch was saved.
The young intern sat by himself in one corner of the coffee shop. He had learned about medicine, but more importantly, he had learned something about life.
