Fit Guide:
It's all in the details

The best way to look like a million bucks is not to spend a million bucks, but to wear a suit that fits well. Once you read these basic tips your eyes will be opened to all the ill-fitting, boxy suits inhabiting the world around you. Use this guide to find your fit, and don’t be afraid to enlist a tailor to make slight alterations so your Pursuit suit fits just right. If you already own suits from some other store, put them on — do they fit like this? If not, don’t panic, Pursuit can help!

pocket

You’ve got a pocket, use it. A pocket square will add a subtle bit of style to your look. Some coat pockets come stitched shut. Carefully use a seam ripper to open it up.

waist

Your coat should gently follow the contour of your midsection with only a little breathing room, but it shouldn’t be so tight that it strains the buttons. Even if you aren’t a slender guy, wearing a coat that closely follows your shape will make you look more slender than a boxy coat. And remember, with a two-button suit, you only use the top button — don’t make the rookie mistake of buttoning both.

jacket length

If your coat is the right length, it should fall to the middle of the palm when your arms rest at your sides, just reaching past the bottom of your zipper on a well-fit pair of pants. A sure-fire way to spot an amateur is a coat that hangs to the knee.

leg width

Flat front pants are the modern look. The alternative, which we DO NOT endorse, are pleats. Pleats are large creases sewn into the pants just below the waist. You won’t find any pleats at Pursuit — we’ll leave those for grandpa’s suit store.

shoulder width

A coat should hug your shoulders snugly. If you can do jumping jacks with the coat buttoned, it’s too loose. If you feel you can only move in robot motions (that will get old quickly), it’s too tight!

jacket flaps

The coat has slits in the back to allow for movement. Be sure your coat isn’t so tight on your backside that the vents are open like a doggy door when you’re standing straight. 

pant length

You don’t want pant legs stuck to you like skinny jeans. Even guys with thicker legs will look better if their pants give just enough room to walk and sit, but don’t flap in the breeze.

sleeve length

Most guys prefer their sleeve to reach just above the bend of the wrist so they can show ¼” to ½” of shirt sleeve. It’s important you don’t end up with a sleeve hanging around your knuckles or a sleeve so short you need to wear three watches to fill the gap. Many guys have a crippling phobia of short sleeves, so they buy a coat that is sized as a ‘long’ when they need a ‘regular’ or a ‘regular’ when they need a ‘short’. Remember a tailor can tweak the length of sleeves, so don’t over-size and end up with a coat dragging around your knees. Follow the sizing guide to help find the proper coat length.

sleeve length

The length of your pants is a matter of preference, but if you have none we recommend a small break like this. You can go little longer if you’re paranoid about showing too much sock when you sit, or a little shorter if you like to show off flashy socks — just be sure you aren’t showing skin when you sit and your pants aren’t doing a ten-car-pile-up on the top of your shoes. Adjusting the length of your pants (the hem) is one of the most basic and affordable alterations a tailor can do. Many tailors will do it in minutes while you wait. If you’re wondering about a cuff, we tend to steer guys away. While small, tight cuffs are somewhat GQ these days, and most cuffs we see around town are far from magazine-worthy. If you’re indifferent, skip the cuff.
