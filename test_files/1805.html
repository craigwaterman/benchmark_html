Girls Soccer: Pressure no problem as Northern Highlands advances to Group 3 state final

Posted on Wednesday, November 18, 2015 1:57 am
by JJ Conrad

LINKS: Cooper Column — Conrad Story — Pedota Photo Gallery

Kayla Klatt gave Northern Highlands the chance to win it a crucial, diving save. And Eva Hurm capitalized on the opportunity with a clutch, clinching goal. But, arguably, no one faced more pressure during the Highlanders’ 6-5 shootout victory after 100 minutes of scoreless soccer against West Morris than the pair of senior All-Americans, Casey Richards and Morgan Busacca.

When their team needed them the most, the Highlanders’ two biggest stars on a team filled with many came through in assertive fashion. Consider the stakes: Northern Highlands entered at 22-0. It has been the nation’s top-ranked team since September. And they have captured the last four Group 3 state titles. Only two teams in state history have ever won five group championships in a row.

But there the Highlanders stood at midfield, all of that in jeopardy while trailing — multiple times — in a penalty kick shootout that could end their season in devastating fashion in the Group 3 semifinals against a formidable opponent. They said afterward they expected a tough, physical game against a solid, defensive-minded squad.

But no one was expecting this.

Tension and suspense filled the air on this cold November night at Ridge High School — with opposing crowds roaring at every goal, save and missed strike. The drama was almost too much to bear for some Highlanders to even watch from the sideline as the nail-biting, eight-round finish took place.

On three separate occasions, Northern Highlands’ season depended on a successful penalty kick. It was the first such situation any of the current Highlanders had been in during a high school game; Highlands had not participated in a PK shootout since the 2009 sectional semifinals against Ramapo. And failure to convert three separate times Tuesday night, and West Morris would be the one moving on to the Group 3 state final on Saturday.

But Northern Highlands would not be denied.

Down, 3-2 in Round 5, Richards went left. Goal. No problem. She admitted this was the more nerve-racking kick.

Down, 4-3 in Round 6, Richards went right this time — her “change-up,” as she described it. It resulted in another season-saving goal — after the teams were permitted to re-adjust their lineup in the now sudden-death style format.

Madigan didn’t have to think twice about sending Richards out for a second consecutive kick. “Casey’s one of our best PK shooters,” Madigan said of the Georgetown-bound Richards. “She’s reliable, she’s smart and she can go either way. She did exactly what we anticipated.”

Down, 5-4 in Round 7, now it was the Fordham-bound Busacca’s turn, as the light-out defender ripped one upper 90. Goal. Again — a third straight strike with Highlands’ back against the wall.

“Sometimes the thought does cross your mind like, ‘Oh, what if I miss?’ ” Busacca said. “But you really just have to block it out. We do this every day in practice and you just have to be confident in your shot and be calm. Being calm is really the main thing.”

As Hurm’s clinching goal trickled in off the hands of West Morris’ goalie, there was calm no more. It was the first time the Highlanders led in the drama-filled, eight-round shootout and a wild celebration — with players, coaches and fans — ensued. The game was over. And Highlands survived its biggest scare of the year.

“There’s just so much emotion in a moment like that,” said Highlands coach Tara Madigan, who sprinted — along with all of the Highlander reserves — from the sideline to the dogpile celebration near the 18 in search of Hurm, who Madigan eventually found at the bottom of the raucous pile.

“It’s so exciting. I’ve been on both sides of these moments and it’s filled with excitement, we’re relieved, we’re enjoying the moment. I’m happy for the girls that they got to experience it like this and happy to be moving on.”

“My heart went from dropping, to fluttering, to experiencing every emotion imaginable,” Richards said. “That game was unreal.”

“All of us just sitting right here were saying how we still can’t believe it,” Busacca said. “My mind is blank right now. I don’t even know what to think. Once Eva’s goal went in, we all just lost it. We just went crazy. It was 100 minutes of energy all pent up and released after Eva’s goal won it.”

A fairly even first half — which included a post hit by West Morris freshman and leading scorer Emily Leyson in the 27th minute off a header on a corner kick — turned into a dominant final 40 minutes of regulation from Northern Highlands, with the Highlanders getting an ample amount of opportunities from Richards, Hurm, Ariel Somple and Amanda Pook. Joanna Kelly was also heavily involved and active in creating scoring chances for Highlands Tuesday night.

Unofficially, West Morris had two shots on goal throughout the entire second half and both overtime periods, one coming on a long-range direct kick and the other a non-threatening look off a corner kick. Aside from that, Highlands’ defense — which has now posted 21 shutouts in 23 games this season and 27 shutouts in their last 29 — again showed why its considered among the best in the state and country.

With Julia Pash and Julia Homan working the interior, Sarah Kelly and Busacca controlling the outside and Klatt posting up confidently in net, West Morris struggled to gain and hold possession or navigate with any efficiency through the top-flight and cohesive unit.

“The game was really so back-and-forth, especially early on,” Richards said. “They were a very good team. Credit to them, they gave us a great game and probably our hardest game all season. We missed chances, they missed chances, but we’re just happy to move on. We’ve thought all along how this is our year and we’re supposed to bounce back after last year [when NH lost its No. 1 national ranking late in the year]. I just think it’d be extremely special to finish this season off with a state title.”

Officially — and somewhat lost in the exuberant postgame celebration — was the fact that Tuesday’s result will go down as a tie. The way the Highlanders celebrated, that fact seemed to be the furthest thing from their minds. But Highlands’ 28-game winning streak did come to an end, though its unbeaten streak moved to 29 straight.

In Tuesday’s most recent poll put out by the NSCAA, Highlands remained in the No. 1 spot nationally, but that was released hours before the state semifinals and subsequent “tie.” The official draw creates a bit of uncertainty now as to whether or not Highlands will claim the national title should it go on to win Saturday’s Group 3 final against Colts Neck and finish 23-0-1 overall. My guess is yes, but a talented Colts Neck team will pose as another viable threat.

But that’s all for another day. Tuesday night belonged to Northern Highlands, as the perennial power again showed its championship fortitude. It showed its poise. It showed its determination. They’re a team that just refuses to lose these days.

“We were definitely thinking, ‘It can’t end like this, we’re not letting it end today,’ ” Busacca said. “We have done so much already and put so much into this season… there’s a reputation for this program and so much at stake. We all know what’s at stake, but we tried not to focus on that. We just wanted to focus on what we needed to do because that’s how we got where we are now.”

Where they are now is back in a familiar place — the Group 3 state finals for the fifth straight year.

It wasn’t easy, but Northern Highlands got it done.

Now there’s just one more left to get.
