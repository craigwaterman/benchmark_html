Truckies battle at bowser too

THINK you are paying too much for fuel?

Spare a thought for those relying on diesel.

Truck drivers and motorists using diesel are also suffering the city-country price discrepancy, with the RACV estimating retail diesel prices are about 20 cents a litre higher than wholesale.

For Border transport companies, that can add up pretty quickly.

Matt Willis, a driver for Wodonga’s D&M Transport, yesterday filled up at the Caltex in Melbourne Road for 128.9 cents a litre.

For an 1100-litre truck that needs filling up twice a week, that’s more than $2800 right there.

Mr Willis said diesel prices had dropped, but there was often a discrepancy of five cents between the city and country.

“It’s a huge cost in operating a truck,” he said.

“For the trucks running to the capital cities regularly though, they can save a bit when they fill up there, but you want to support your locals as well.”

Mr Willis estimated the recent price drop would save a company running four trucks between $8000 and $10,000 a month.

But RACV manager of vehicle engineering Michael Case said the savings could — and should — be even greater.

“The current wholesale price for diesel is 106.2 cents per litre and the price is continuing to drop,” he said.

“These prices are more than 20 cents higher than the wholesale price. This huge price difference cannot be justified.

“Frankly, diesel drivers in Melbourne and especially regional Victoria are being ripped off.”

Mr Case said the price at the pump should be at least 10 cents a litre less.

Driver fined $2142 after truck gets stuck in Galston Gorge

 

One of the warning signs for trucks on Galston Rd, Hornsby.

THE driver whose truck got stuck in Galston Gorge yesterday ignored the warnings of his passenger and other motorists.

Ku-ring-gai Local Area Command crime prevention officer Paul Cleary said other road users were again inconvenienced by a “rogue truck driver”.

“Despite protests from his passenger that the truck was over length and won’t make it and the fact car drivers behind him were frantically flashing headlights and beeping him, the driver went for it and lost,” he said.

The length of the truck was 11.2m, which is way over the length restriction in the gorge of 7.5m.

The driver was fined $2142 and has lost six demerit points. The truck’s registration has also been cancelled.

The latest incident follows a number of other problems with oversized vehicles getting stuck in the gorge last year, including a number of trucks, a bus and a car towing a caravan.

Satellite phones a must for remote work: TWU

Union says stranded WA truck driver would be alive if he’d been carrying equipment worth less than $1,000.

The TWU says truck drivers should be required to carry satellite phones and EPIRBs for all remote journeys.

The Transport Workers Union (TWU) says a truck driver who became stranded on a roadside in remote Western Australia and died could have been saved if he’d been carrying more appropriate communications equipment.

Clayton Miller was found dead about 20km from his bogged truck last week.

He had been delivering tanks to a remote station in the state’s north-west and died searching for help in 46-degree heat.

TWU assistant secretary for WA Paul Aslan says current laws require employers to supply lone workers with "up-to-date" means of communication for use in emergency situations.

He has urged law makers to specify satellite phones be carried for all remote areas journeys, along with emergency position indicating radio beacons [EPIRBs].

"There’s absolutely no doubt this man would be alive today if he had been carrying those devices," he says.

The satellite phone allows for real time communication with emergency services while an EPIRB offers a more reliable positioning service than standard global positioning devices. Both devices have become much cheaper in recent years.

WA Police has reiterated the importance of staying with the vehicle when trapped in remote areas.

"If people end up in this type of predicament we advise them to try not to panic, and stay with their vehicle at all times," it says.

"It is much easier for searchers to spot a vehicle from the air, than it is a person."

Nurse shines light on hard life of truckies

RECOGNITION: Juanita Johnson has been nominated for the Heritage Volunteer of the Year award for her work with the Lights on the Hill organisation.

A TRAGIC accident seven years ago changed the direction of Juanita Johnson's life forever.

In January, 2008, her 11-year-old son and his father were tragically killed in a head-on truck crash after the driver of the other vehicle fell asleep at the wheel.

That year, she attended the Lights on the Hill Trucking Memorial convoy where the names of her loved ones were added to the memorial wall.

The mourning mother soon realised she wanted to give back to the community and use her losses to help others.

A beauty therapist by trade, she began studying as a nurse at the University of Southern Queensland and now specialises in the palliative cancer ward at the Toowoomba Hospital.

She also started volunteering for the Lights on the Hill organisation, soon becoming the treasurer and then the secretary of the committee.

Ms Johnson's tireless fundraising efforts and work to honour and raise awareness for our truckies led to her nomination for the Heritage Bank Volunteer of the Year Award.

But the mother and grandmother is more interested in those she helps than recognition.

"It's very humbling, but I just do it to try and help save a life. More than 600 truck drivers are killed on Australian roads every year. If that was any other industry, there would be investigations, but nothing is being done to improve what's going on," she said.

"You cannot downland bread and milk - everything at some stage has been on a truck.

"It's a very difficult job and they can see very difficult things. They're often the first at a fatal and don't get counselling.

"People need to realise although it's a big vehicle; it's a human behind the wheel with a family they want to get home to."

Veteran truckie, Hans Peter Hansen, laid to rest

Peter Hansen.

>CAR and truck horns from the Peak Downs Hwy occasionally interrupted the solemn tone at Hans Peter Hansen's funeral at Walkerston Cemetery.

The beloved truckie was laid to rest amid glowing tributes to his character, work ethic and friendliness.

The passers-by were tooting their horns to acknowledge the service, held yesterday from 10am.

"He'd give you the shirt off his back," a work colleague emphasised while delivering a speech.

"Every day, he lifted those around him."

Dressed in black, friends and family paid their final respects to the man affectionately known as "Old Fella".

But it was Mr Hansen's buoyant spirit that many spoke of - never shirking a hard day's work and always there to help friends and family.

Finally at rest, Mr Hansen was farewelled with a fishing rod on his coffin. He was a keen fisherman and camper.

Mr Hansen was also remembered as a man who loved his daughter, Rachael, her partner and his grandchildren.

Well-known in truck driving circles, Mr Hansen worked across the country in various roles.

Born in Brisbane, he moved to Maleny at 14, and worked on a dairy farm, among other jobs.

His career would take him throughout Australia where his "no frills" attitude earned him many friends.

At the time of his death, he worked for JAT Refrigerated Road Services.

The circumstances of Mr Hansen's death weighed heavily on mourners' minds but it didn't stem their glowing praise for the 72-year-old.

His death not only touched friends and family, it also shocked the Mackay community.

Mr Hansen died on December 14 following an alleged assault the previous day.

An 18-year-old man has been charged with murder over his death.

Truck licence restrictions 'impractical'

 

Truck drivers from across Australia could be banned from using Adelaide's South Eastern Freeway. Photo: Getty

A call for truckies from any state to master a tricky South Australian freeway descent before they can get a licence is impractical, the industry says.

SA deputy coroner Anthony Schapel on Monday made a string of recommendations in his findings into the deaths of two truck drivers in separate crashes at the bottom of Adelaide's South Eastern Freeway.

The coroner recommended that no heavy vehicle licence be issued to any person unless they have demonstrated they can safely negotiate the freeway descent in the presence of a trained instructor.

"These requirements should apply not only to licensing regimes in South Australia, but should be a requirement that is also imposed on interstate drivers," he said.

South Australian Road Transport Association director Steve Shearer says the idea is unworkable.

"We have always supported the notion of appropriate training in hills, but what do you do to the career prospects of somebody who works on the west coast," he told reporters.

"They're never going to come to Adelaide. They drive in a dead flat environment and always will. These things sound simple but they're not all simple."

The industry group broadly supported the coroner's recommendations but some would be difficult to implement, Mr Shearer said.

The coroner recommended that heavy vehicle drivers negotiating the freeway descent for the first time be accompanied and supervised by a trained and experienced driver.

Interstate drivers should be required to have "a certificate of demonstrated competence" and to maintain a log in relation to all descents on the freeway.

He also called for an increase in penalties to include prison terms for drivers who do not use arrester beds when their truck is out of control or who do not use the correct gear.

The coroner delivered the recommendations in his findings into the deaths of truck drivers John Posnakidis and James William Venning.

Mr Posnakidis, 42, died in October 2010, while waiting at a bus stop for assistance.

He was killed when an out-of-control semi-trailer, driven by a Victorian truckie, hurtled down the freeway and hit him near the same intersection.

Mr Venning, 41, died in January 2014 when he crashed into a wall at the major intersection at the beginning of the freeway.

Transport Minister Stephen Mullighan said the government supported the recommendations and was already in the process of implementing some suggestions.

CFA crews had to extinguish flames in a rubbish that was dumped in the Seaford Hotel car park this morning. Picture: Lisa Edgerton

“There were no injuries or damage to property and the waste is in the process of being collected and will be disposed of appropriately,’’ he said.

Northern Territory trucking companies welcome cheaper diesel, but no relief for aviation industry

The Northern Territory transport industry has welcomed lower diesel costs, but the aviation industry has missed out.

The average price of diesel in the Northern Territory has dropped by about 34 cents since October last year to a current price of about $1.41 per litre.

When fuel costs were high Darwin based trucking company Road Trains of Australia introduced an extra charge on customers, called a fuel levy.

This month it dropped its fuel levy that it has been charging customers since 2009.

Mark Castagna, operations manager for Tanami Transport, a livestock carrier that operates out of Alice Springs, said when fuel prices were at their peak, diesel made up around two thirds of the company's running costs.

"When you've got a road train that does 850 metres to the litre, it hurts alright," Mr Castagna said.

Tanami Transport is yet to lower its rates due to the lack of work for livestock carriers during the wet season.

But Mr Castagna said he might reassess that.

"The fuel may have dropped but everything else hasn't dropped, like tyres and spare parts," he said.

"So you've got to look at everything, not just the fuel, but definitely the fuel will make a difference."

Aviation fuel costs remain high

Lower diesel prices have not translated into lower costs for aviation fuel, avgas.

Katherine Aviation general manager Kerrie Mott said she has not seen a significant price decrease.

"From our perspective, nothing has been passed on to us in any significant format and, until that is the case, we can't forward any savings on to our customers," she said.

Katherine Aviation purchases avgas for their remote area bases like Groote Eylandt, Borroloola and Kalkaringi.

"You can only really get fuel to [the remote bases] in the dry season, usually the last delivery you can get out is in October," Ms Mott said.

"After the last wet season it was June before we could get significant quantities of bulk fuel to our bases.

"Once the remote fuel is utilised, and we bring in a new shipment of fuel, then we may see a significant drop, but until that happens we are business as usual."

West Melbourne truck curfews in place next week

New restrictions for trucks in Footscray and Yarraville will apply from the beginning of the new school year.

New, extended truck curfews in residential areas of Melbourne’s inner-west will apply from Thursday, January 29.

Those ignoring the new rules will run the risk of an on-the-spot fine of $148.

VicRoads says the curfews restricting truck access to Moore Street and Somerville Road in Yarraville and Footscray "strike a balance for the local community and local truck operators".

"The curfews will see a reduction of trucks using local streets and address community concerns around safety, noise and air quality," VicRoads says.

Coinciding with the beginning of the new school year, the curfews mean heavy vehicles will be unable to access Moore Street between 8pm and 6am weeknights, and over the weekend from 1pm Saturdays until 6am Mondays.

The existing curfew on Somerville Road will be extended to add school drop-off and pick-up times to the times trucks will be excluded. In addition to the same hours as the Moore Street curfew, trucks will be banned on Somerville Road between 8am and 9.30am, and from 2.30pm to 4pm Monday to Friday.

Exemptions will be granted to transport vehicles servicing local businesses.

The Victorian Transport Association says this concession was hard-fought for, and it is important that the industry abides by the curfew arrangements.

"As an industry, we need to ensure that compliance to the exemptions is of the highest level and [in] doing so demonstrate the importance of maintaining access while still accepting the concerns of the community," the VTA says.

VicRoads will host an information session* for transport operators and other affected businesses tomorrow.

Firefighters who were injured during a rescue at a truck rollover and ammonium nitrate explosion last year will receive bravery awards for their selfless efforts.

Lieutenants Jake Sullivan and Peter Hackwood will receive the Queensland Fire and Emergency Services Commissioner’s Medal for Valour, and Nathan Thompson and Clinten McCarthy will receive the Commissioner’s Commendation for Bravery Award, at a ceremony on Monday next week.
