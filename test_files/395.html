Mother's Day: The Importance of Us

This day has become so much more to me than I ever intended for it to be.

Years ago, it was a day set aside to honor my truly fabulous mom.
She is the mother of all moms.
In every memory I have of my mom she makes motherhood look like a cakewalk.
She's just that good at it.
And the thing that I think makes her so good is all the grace that flows from her.
As a child I found myself constantly stepping into her puddles of grace, and I would soak it up, anxious for the day that I could squeeze some of that grace onto my own children.
When Mother's Day would roll around I'd find myself gathering together fancy paper and glue and markers and heading into the woods (literally found in our backyard) searching for the perfect wildflower. I'd sit down and use my tools and treasures to create a gift worthy of my mom.

Now, as a mother of three, I see that this day is not as simple as construction paper and glue and, wild flowers from the woods tied with pieces of string.

A very dear friend of mine lost her mom, her treasure, a few months ago, way too young and to an awful disease. My friend is broken, and this day, a day that had always been full of the puddles of grace and joy that her mother poured out, will forever be muddied and messy.

About eight months ago I met a woman who lost her son suddenly. He was 10 and he simply didn't wake up one morning. May 18th will be three years since that happened. I was talking with her yesterday and she said that she kind of holds her breath through this month just waiting for it to be over. I can't even begin to imagine the depths of her pain.

As we began our journey into parenthood we quickly and painfully learned that just wanting to be a mother is not enough. You cannot force yourself to get pregnant, so much of it is out of our hands. It was here in my story that Mother's Day began to warp a little. I still had the honor of celebrating my mom, but year after year I became desperate to be a mom, and as I stuffed a card in an envelope and licked the edge, the bitter taste that I never seemed to notice before was pungent on my tongue.

Oh how I longed to be the one celebrated on this day.

And here I am! That chapter of my story is over and I am somewhere in the beginning of the chapter titled "Motherhood" or "This Wild Ride" or "I Pooped I need to Wipe!"
And I get so much more than a card on this day. I get sweet little handmade vases and birdhouses and pillows sewn and stuffed by sweet little hands. I'm woken up by giggles and kisses and sweet munchkins ripping into the wrapping paper they just helped to tape down exhilarated about showing me what they picked out when they went shopping with their daddy (This years gift includes a jar of Nutella, my love language.) And I get to snuggle up with Macyn, Truly and August and give them as many kisses as I want. Being a mother has filled me to overflowing. There is a love I find in my children that I never knew existed. Living this love out day after day was well worth the wait. Being Macyn, Truly and August's mom is the gift of my life! THE GIFT OF MY LIFE!

But as I said, this day contains more than I ever thought it would.

All three of my kids are mine because they cannot be another mother's.

Today, three different women, who mothered my children for nine months and I'm sure think about them every single day, wake up with a void. Their arms are empty and mine are full. The tragedy and the magnitude and the beauty of that are not lost on me. Their loss is my gain and today, a day in which we celebrate mothers, magnifies that, for them too I'm sure.

So I wake up on this day with conflicting feelings in my heart. I think about the loss of my fertility and those who are once again childless this mother's day. I think about people who have lost their moms and mothers who have lost a child. I think about birthmothers and sacrifice and empty arms. I think about the pain that this day evokes.

And I think about the joy. I think about my own mom. Oh how I love her so. I can never thank her enough for the example she has been to me and the way in which she raised me. I wake up on this morning full of celebration for her. I think about myself as a mom. I can not believe that I get to parent the kids I parent. I wake up on this day honored and thrilled to be celebrated and so full of joy and completeness. Mother's Day really is a great day for me.

As I reflect on all that this day means to so many different people, I have learned that it isn't really about me or you, but more about us. On days like today that are difficult and exciting, painful and full of joy I think about the us. I think about how I don't have to give up the joy and celebration that this day holds for me, but I am also leaning the importance of recognizing the pain that this day may stir up in another person. I'm learning to sit in the middle of things. To snuggle my kids and laugh at the joy they bring me, and to wrap my arms around my friend and cry with her in the midst of her pain. Because I don't think it is about me today, I think it is about all of us. The weaving of stories and joy and pain and love. Me learning from you and you learning from me. I'd lack so much without my mother, but then again, I'd be lacking without birthmothers and the pain of infertility and the awesome choices of my grandmothers and the people around me who have and continue to shape my views and show me how to love and let me suffer with them and who come along side me and suffer with me and celebrate with me.

So today I wish all the moms out there a very happy Mother's Day. I celebrate with you and I bask in the celebration happening around me.
And to those who are hurting and find themselves in the mud today, I embrace you and say I am sorry and I love you. I will walk this with you,
because I need you,
and you may need me,
and we all need us!

The Power of Your Story

Stories.

We've all got them.

Our choices, our life circumstances, the choices made by others.
The stranger who went above and beyond; the family member who went too far; the luck we had that one fateful day;the hard work put into something worth fighting for.

All of these events make up our story.
And where ever you are right now, your life, your story is unfolding.

Some of us are working hard to make our story what we believe it should be, what we believe is the best of the best. Most of us find ourselves falling into our story as it happens to us; one seemingly insignificant decision at a time.

I think it is good to stop ourselves once in awhile and see where we are at in our story. To hold ourselves accountable for the choices we've made and to see how the choices of others are affecting us. To look around at others in the midst of their own story and check to see how we are affecting one another.

Today I had the honor and privilege of having some of the best story tellers'  feet under my table. People who make a living telling their story and seeing the benefit and glory in the stories of others. People who have told their own stories either through writing books or writing music, and understand the power of ones' story. My time with these professional story tellers got me thinking about my own story. They got me thinking about he stories of the people I love and the strangers I meet. They reminded me of the importance and power of ones' story. And the fact that our stories can only have an impact if we tell them.

I think about my kids. About their stories. How the decisions of their birthmothers led them to me. How I made a decision to make them my own; how they will make decisions on how to respond to our decisions and how all of this will come together, like a beautiful mosaic. Each decision made is a shard of glass and tile and ceramic, and as these pieces are picked up and placed together they will have to potential to become beautiful and powerful stories.

Ones story is a powerful thing.

I think about those whose views I oppose. The people who wake up everyday and make choices that bewilder me. Choices that may effect me and the ones I love negatively. Choices that go against everything I believe in. I can be so quick to push my opinions on them, but have I stopped long enough hear their story?

I think about my recent trip to Israel. I think about the conflict between Israelis and Palestinians. I think about the wrongs done to both people and the strong views and opinions each person on each side hold tight to. And I wonder, have they stopped long enough to hear each others' stories? Have WE here in the West, stopped long enough to hear their stories?

I think about all the race mess going on all over the United States. I think about black men and white cops. I think about choices made and opinions imposed and rights violated and humanity at stake. And I wonder, have they stopped long enough to hear each others' stories?

I think about my own family. I think about my Macyn Hope, everyday walking to a school where she is the minority. I think about the parents dropping off their typical kids, having no idea that Macyn is there. I think about what is best for Macyn and all the kids at her school. But have I stopped to tell our story? Or even more important, have I stopped to listen to theirs'?

IT IS IMPOSSIBLE TO HATE SOMEONE ONCE YOU KNOW THEIR STORY.

My wise, kind, gracious father always talks about the gift of putting our feet under the table. I love this. I love that he has instilled in me the importance of taking the time to put my feet under the table with other people. And I want to take it a step further and challenge you all to do the same. I want to think about what would happen if we put our feet under the table with people and simply listened to their story. What if we looked at that black kid looting on the street and simply asked him his story? What if we sat across from a white police officer and simply asked him his story? What if we found that person in our life who rubs us the wrong way or disagrees with everything we believe in our has greatly offended us and we put our feet under the table with them and sat and listened to their story?
The power behind this simple act is of a greatness we cannot begin to wrap our minds around. Because friends, I truly believe:

IT IS IMPOSSIBLE TO HATE SOMEONE ONCE YOU KNOW THEIR STORY.

What is Beauty


When did we as humans decide that beauty is the most important thing?

You may argue that it is in our nature due to evolution. Just look at the animal kingdom, in most species males are the most beautiful gender so that they can more easily attract a female so that their bloodline can live on. Even the animal kingdom holds beauty to the highest regard. You may argue that without beauty living things would fail to exist.

My friends, we are human beings, we are not animals and I'd like to think that just as in basically every area of life we should also be superior to animals in the way we view beauty, or rather the way we view the less than beautiful.

I have three children who I happen to think are the most beautiful kids ever in the whole wide world. I know that kids are always beautiful in their parents eyes.
But not necessarily in the eyes of society.

My middle daughter is a stunner. She happens to be beautiful to me as well as most people in society. Not an outing goes by that someone doesn't comment on her beauty. She is unarguably stunning!

But to be honest friends, enough with the "your son/daughter/child is so beautiful."
To that I think to myself, "that's nice".  Just today when we were dropping Macyn off at school Truly ran up to a preschooler who has Down syndrome and gave her a big huge hug. The woman dropping her off (her mom?) said of Truly, "wow, she's so beautiful." And I know that, and it is true, but it was the sweet compassionate part of her that ran up to this little girl and gave her a hug, not her beauty. Truly Star is so much more than beautiful and I want her to be known for so much more. I want her to be noticed for her kindness and compassion, for her joyful demeanor for her patience and sweetness, for her ability to problem solve and her quick wit. When was the last time you complemented someone's child for one of those characteristics? I believe that if these kinds of characteristics were held in high regard then people would in fact notice them and even be looking for them. When I think about what I want for Truly's future, who I want her to be as an adult, I can think of so many characteristics I pray she possesses, and beauty doesn't even make the list.

I admit to being as guilty as the next guy here. I always find myself noticing beautiful people and being drawn in by their beauty. I complement beautiful kids and babies for their beauty all the time, but I'm working on it and trying to be intentional about commenting on the more important characteristics I see in them.

Why is beautiful so important?

My eldest daughter is a different kind of beautiful. We do not get comments on her beauty everywhere we go. It is not rare for people to comment on how beautiful she is, but it is more common to get strange stares and uncomfortable awkward moments. Macyn  is quite different looking. He eyes are too almond and her ears too little, her face is too flat and her tongue is too big. She is too different for the "beautiful" that has been forced upon us all. It is not uncommon for Truly to be told how beautiful she is and Macyn to be completely overlooked.

But man oh man, she's a babe through and through. She is overflowing with joy and wants to share it with everyone she meets. She is patient and persistent and has the best sense of humor. She loves with abandon and judges no one, she is prone to contentment and finds joy in the smallest of things.

Yet on paper she is what society does not want.
But in my life she is the goal.

God says that we are "fearfully and wonderful made...knit together in our mother's womb."

Are we to look at people with Down syndrome or Cerebral Palsy or missing limbs or in a wheelchair or with distorted faces or plain old ugly faces and say that they are not included in this part of God's word?! Are these people not also fearfully and wonderfully made? When did we get to set the standard for what is "wonderful"? How bold and arrogant and foolish of us to think that we can even come close to what God considers to be "wonderful".

The other day I was at a conference and one of the speakers asked us to think about the things that break our hearts. And after much thought I'll tell you what breaks my heart:
What breaks my heart is seeing Macyn as an outsider. It breaks my heart that the majority of people, especially kids her age fail to notice her or even worse, avoid her. It breaks my heart that so many people with special needs are avoided and unnoticed because they do not reflect what society has deemed as important. They are not the kind of beautiful that we have been told we should strive to be. In fact there is something in us that has been taught to avoid these kinds of people at all costs. And that breaks me. It breaks my heart how difficult it is for people who are different looking to make friends and to be pursued.

But I do have so much hope, always so much hope. There are people working hard to break the cycle of what is considered beautiful. There is currently a whole campaign going on called "Changing The Face Of Beauty" in which companies everywhere are seeing the true beauty and value in people who are differently abled and using them to represent their brand.  And I encourage all of you to hop on board. I encourage you to think about what it is you find to be beautiful. What it is you are teaching your children . I encourage you, and myself, to look around at the people you choose to spend time with and see how many of them have special needs. Trust me, kids with special needs are learning at your schools, playing at your parks, walking in your neighborhoods and going to your churches. Talk with your kids about what really makes someone beautiful and make a great friend while you are at it. Encourage businesses around you to include people with special needs in their advertising.

Ask questions and be honest. It may be super uncomfortable to have a conversation with someone with Cerebral Palsy because he/she may have thick slow speech that is difficult to understand. Be honest with that person and tell them you want to know more about them but they will need to be patient with you because you have a difficult time understanding them at times. People with disabilities are usually very aware of what makes them different so you don't really need to beat around the bush. Always be respectful and treat people as your peer, not as someone less superior.
I always appreciate when people just ask me about the best way to respond to a situation; such as when their innocent child points at Macyn and says something along the lines of "Why is her tongue sticking out like that?" and the mom or dad say, "Let's go ask her what her name is and we can see if she or her mommy can answer that for us." Trust me, that kind of approach is so much more respectful than avoidance. The person with the disability and/or his/her parents notice EVERY TIME a person tries to avoid them or avoid their child's uncomfortable comments and questions.

These are just some of the things I've been thinking about a lot lately. I don't speak for anyone but myself but I happen to know I am not alone in these thoughts. I love all of you who read this blog and journey with us on Instagram. I'm sure the difference we are making will surface in a big way sooner than we think. Thanks for being apart of The Lucky Few.

Israel

Reflecting at the Sea Of Galilee 

I’ve been home from Israel for two weeks.

I still can’t believe that I got to go on this trip.

And as it goes with trips like this, I’ve come home not the same; something in me is altered.
It’s not like a huge life change, not a complete wardrobe modification,
but a little more subtle more like the hem of a sleeve, or better yet a new button on my pants letting them out to make room for more  (figuratively and literally, can anyone say pita and hummus?)

I’ve had a few conversations with my people on this side of Israel. People who I wish I could have shared the trip with, people who took care of things on this side of the globe while I was gone. Every time I begin to tell stories or answer questions or recall events, I realize that more time will be needed to share what I want to share, and that there is not one moment or even a dozen that I can say are the best…the whole trip was the best.

I went on this trip with a group called The Telos Group (find out more about them here (Telos Group). I’m so thankful that my first look at Israel was through this group. I learned SOOOO much! And if there’s one word that kept coming to mind day after day and conversation after conversation it’s complicated. Whew…things in Israel and Palestine are complicated. The history, the present and the future are so complicated. On this trip we got to learn about Israel from the lens of so many different people and groups; all of whom love the land they live in, all of whom have wounds that run deep. And we got to hear it from the mouths of the people themselves, not from a third party biased news station. One morning we listened to the life experiences of a man who is the leader of a Palestine refugee camp in the West Bank. Later in the trip we met with women, both Israeli and Palestinian whose common thread is the loss of a loved one in the mess going on in this beautiful land. Women who have said enough is enough and who have devoted their lives to seeing peace between the Israeli and Palestinian people. And another morning we found ourselves at a lookout on the boarder of Gaza learning the perspective of an Israeli  man who leads the military defense on the boarder of Gaza. We had a life giving Shabbat dinner on the Sabbath in the home of a jovial and kind Orthodox Jew and we spent time at a University in the West Bank that is a place for young Palatines to learn the arts and the importance of peace. And this my friends is just scratching the surface. 

To add to the fact that I was in Israel meeting the people, walking the streets, eating the food, I was experiencing this with a group of 25 women.  And not just any 25 women but top notch high caliber women. I would have been honored to have one meal with any of these women, let alone three a day for an entire week. And as it goes on trips like this, you know the life changing kind, the people who started out as strangers feel like family now. I miss them so terribly and I’m mad at them for living states away. 

As I mentioned before I can’t think of one moment that stands out as the highlight, nor can I say that I’m bringing home one main lesson learned. But seeing that this is a blog post and not a novel I have been trying to think about one thing I want to share if I can only share one thing:

As I said, things in Israel/Palestine are very, very complicated. I ashamedly admit that before this trip I knew close to nothing about this part of the world, expect that it’s important that I know something. This part of the world birthed Jesus, the cornerstone of my faith and all that I hope to live for. That I knew! And now I feel like I know as much as I can possibly learn in one weeks time. And while I’ve learned so much the piece I bring with me to share with all you is a universal truth, one I try to live by and one that was confirmed even further for me during my time in Israel/Palestine and it’s this:
Everything changes when we remember that people are people. Everyone has a story and the importance of sitting across the table listening to the stories of those who are or have the potential to be our enemy is critical…CRITICAL for any kind of peace in any area of life! Nothing is going to get done and no peace will be known if people continue to say how wronged they have been and continue to avoid relationships with the ones who disagree and make us uncomfortable. I believe this is true for Israel/Palestine and I believe this is true for race issues in America and I believe this is true for people who homeschool or choose not to, and I believe this is true for republicans and democrats and everyone in between. 
And don’t get me wrong, it is not about compromising your convictions and beliefs. Not in the least. But I think it’s time we ask about what is more important, people or our opinions? People or justice? People or comfort? And don’t hear what I’m not saying. I have very strong convictions and beliefs that I know many of you disagree with, and I am not willing to compromise them, but I am willing to agree to disagree, and I am willing to hear you out and develop relationships with people who believe differently than I do and who make me think about why I believe what I do and live the way I do. And I’m willing to love people who believe differently than me. I know that my life is richer because of all that I have learned from people who look differently, act differently, eat differently and vote differently than I do. 

It’s that idea that I’ve written about before about Jesus teaching us to pray for God’s kingdom to come here on earth as it is in heaven, and what do we know about this kingdom? We know that it’s all kinds of people there, from every place in the world, speaking every language. We know that it is not just rich, white, republican men. And hey, there is nothing wrong with being rich, white, republican and male; I know a lot of that type and I love them dearly! It’s about stepping outside of our worlds getting dirty and uncomfortable, posturing ourselves with a mantel of humility and possibly becoming more like Jesus as we are molded and stretched and as we learn from people on the opposite end.

So when it comes to Israel/Palestine where do I stand?
Well I stand for Israel,
And I stand for Palestine,
I stand for People,
I stand for Love,

I stand for Peace. 

And for me, there's nothing complicated about that.

World Down Syndrome Day 2015

I love the idea of a Secret Garden.
 I remember being a kid and hearing about the story "The Secret Garden" and having my first introduction to its magic and charm. I remember how I would close my eyes and imagine finding a garden like the one in the book. As a kid the thing that stuck with me the most was the magic and the beauty and refuge that the garden encompassed.  Oh how I wanted to spend just a day in that place. 

 As an adult who has failed many attempts at a successful garden I can look back on The Secret Garden and the pragmatist in me pushes through the magic and reminds me of the realities of maintaining a garden. Gardening is no joke. A successful garden requires hard work and determination. The beauty and the fruit that a garden has the potential to provide come at a cost: early mornings spent weeding and pruning and fertilizing; hot afternoons with only the shade of a wide brim hat; harvesting and picking and canning and preserving. 

 Yes, it is a lot of work.
But great is the reward.

 Nothing tastes better than food fresh from a garden that you toiled over. 
A secret garden, one that requires hard work rewarded by new growth and beauty is the kind of place that I want to invite all my friends to. I want everyone I love sitting around a table enjoying the fresh produce and breathing in the scents of the fragrant flowers. It is the kind of place I could never keep to myself.

 Parenting a child with Down syndrome makes me think about a Secret Garden.
It makes me think about hard work. About committing my time and energy to a cause. About early mornings, long afternoons and late nights, spent cutting away weeds and thorns so the flowers can thrive. Parenting a child with Down syndrome is like finding a secret garden. A magical place full of more beauty than my eyes can contain. A place that requires hard work with huge reward.
And now that I'm here, living among the roses and the vines, the butterflies and the worms all I want to do is share it with everyone I love and everyone I come in contact with. Because the truth is, a garden like this should never be kept a secret.

 So friends, please take my hand, and the hands of so many and follow us into the forest, down the path and through the gate. What awaits you may not be what you originally expected, but it is far grander than anything you have yet to discover.

Armenian Baby Born with Down Syndrome

"Dad refuses to give up newborn son with Down syndrome after wife leaves him."

This is the headline making its way around the world.
A number of @macymakesmyday follower and followers of the blog as well as friends of mine have brought this story to my attention. Probably because I have two kiddos with Down syndrome, and probably because one of them, Macyn, is 100% Armenian. 

If you are not familiar with this news story it goes something like this:
A baby boy (Leo) is born in Armenia to an Armenian woman and her husband (Samuel) who is from New Zealand. The baby happens to have Down syndrome. The mother makes the decision to give the baby up and the father says no way, I'm keeping him.

You can find more here: http://abcnews.go.com/Lifestyle/dad-refuses-give-newborn-son-syndrome/story?id=28756025

This story is so heart-breaking and beautiful all at the same time. And I have some thoughts on the matter (imagine that!) 

First, I'm so thankful for baby Leo, all of us who have someone with Ds in our lives know what a gift this sweet boy is. And I'm thankful for his dad who is brave enough to go against social norms and to say this baby is worth it, even when his society says otherwise. I'm thankful for stories like this that bring attention to the fact the majority of the world still thinks a Down syndrome diagnosis is a terrible thing and that we can have conversations about how wrong this majority is and shed a little more light on the truth about people with Ds. I'm thankful for the outcry of support for Samuel and the love for little Leo. 

This news story is shedding a light on how many Armenians feel about people born with special needs and the actions that they feel forced to take because of the social pressure and lack of support. I can't imagine the kind of pressure this mother felt for her to choose to give up the child she carried for nine months and no doubt loved deeply. My heart breaks for her and her lose.  

Now before I step up on my soapbox (that's right, I haven't even begun!) I must touch on the fact that Macyn is 100% Armenian. Her birthparents were living in the states for less than 10 years when Macyn was born. They chose an adoption plan for Macyn for many reason, some I am aware of, others I will never know. And I want to be clear here that they are wonderful people. We have spent a great deal of time with Macyn's whole birth family, all of whom are very much Armenian and they love and cherish Macyn, and we love and cherish them. 

As this news story has created a worldwide buzz there is much criticism of Armenia and Leo's mother. For us in the USofA I think it is a case of seeing the plank in our own eyes before taking the speck out of the eyes of our Armenian friends. 

You see, we are not much different. How would you feel if you found out that your child would be born with Down syndrome? And as you think about your answer remember that we live in a country that is incredibly supportive of people with Down syndrome. The second a child with DS is born you have teams of people at your disposal to help with everything you can imagine. So how would you feel? How do we feel when we learn the news that our child has Down syndrome? 

Because I have two children with Down syndrome I'd say I'm pretty plugged into the Ds community. In this community I've meet many parents from many different walks of life, who have a child, or two, with Down syndrome and here is how they felt when they first got the news:
shocked
awful
terrified
heart-broken
angry
sad
depressed
You get the picture. 

Some of the women I know who have very young children are still dealing with some of these emotions, I have only love for them, no judgement found here.

Okay here's the good part. I interact with dozens of families daily who have a child with Down syndrome, and while their initial reactions were sadness and terror, now that they have a child with Ds as their own they feel the exact opposite. EVERY SINGLE family I talk to or hear from on a daily basis is thrilled that they get to parent a child with Down syndrome. These are the only people who truly know what it means for their child to have a diagnosis of Down syndrome. These (we) are the only families who should get a voice when talking about what it means to have a child with Ds. If our voices were the only ones heard every time a woman found out her child had Down syndrome, then I have a sneaky suspicion that our society as a whole would begin to change it's mind.

But our voices are being drowned out by the voice of a society who shouts ignorance and fear for all to hear. Those of us parenting a child with Ds, those of us who know what a gift a child with Ds really is are not sitting quiet. Lots of us are sharing our knowledge with the world, but our voices are growing horse because people YOU ARE NOT LISTENING. You may think, yes we are Heather. I love your kids and love people with Down syndrome, and yet I ask again, how would you feel if you found out that your child has Down syndrome? 

All of us are afraid of the unknown and unplanned. 
But the awsome-ness of people with Down syndrome is not unknown, so don't fear my friends!

I'm so humbled by the response we have gotten from all our @macymakesmyday followers as well as the response here on the blog. I can't tell you how many of you have said that you used to be afraid of finding out your child has Down syndrome but after getting to know Macyn and August you no longer are. Amazing people! Thanks for listening.

As I've said many times before on this blog, when it comes to people with Down syndrome in our society we have come a long way, but we have so much further to go. 

As I take the time and energy to write this blog and to post daily on our Instagram account, as I make the choice to share my children with the world, it is not to hear my own voice or for Insta fame, it is because I know that if a change is gunna happen I'm going to have to speak. And I'm honored to be speaking up with many other amazing families who have children with Down syndrome and who also see the importance of their voice. It is my hope and prayer that as we speak up together our voice will become too loud to ignore, and as couples find out that their child has a diagnosis of Down syndrome, they will feel joy and excitement instead of anger and fear.

I don't call it The Lucky Few for nothing.

Chaotic Love

I almost completely forget how difficult and painful my infertility journey was. It’s like waking up from a bad dream and then trying to explain it to someone. The more you explain the less it makes sense and the more you realize how much you forgot and you know that when you were dreaming it everything seemed so real and clear and terrifying, but as you open your eyes and step into the light of a new day the events in your nightmare start to become foggy and blurry and by the time you’re done trying to explain this bad dream you can hardly remember what happened or why it was bad at all.

Has that ever happened to you?

Today at church we sang a song with the lyrics:
“Chaotic love has messed me up, so I can live again.”

And as we sang the song I thought about Macyn.

I thought about years ago when she first entered our lives and interrupted our plans for a healthy baby, a way out of the pain of infertility, an easy and “normal” future for our family.

She showed up, uninvited with holes in her heart; a cannula in her nose because her lungs were too sick to provide the oxygen she needed; a number of medications administered daily, a list of doctors and specialists she needed to see regularly; the possibility of a significantly shortened life…a baby surrounded by chaos. 

She entered my life like pop rock candy washed down with Mountain Dew. 

It’s as though God saw the lifelessness that my infertility journey had imposed on me.
He looked at it and shouted in my face,
“NORMAL LOVE WON'T DO!”
Then drenched me in His chaotic love and it messed me up, so I could once more live.

He gave me Macyn, and it messed me up. 

It messed up my ideas of what a family should look like. It messed up my plans for my future. It messed up the way that I loved narrowly and shallow. It messed up my idea of what my life should look like. It messed up my lack of faith. It messed up my dwindling hope. It messed up my broken heart. It messed up the hold my infertility had on me. It messed up my inability to slow down enough to really see and know God. 

The chaotic love God gave us in Macyn messed me up, and gave me life. 

It made me a person who stands before God screaming not just for His love but for His chaotic love to find me and mess me up so I can be who He wants me to be, not who I think I want to be, or who I think I should be.


I’m so thankful for a God who keeps on messing things up.
