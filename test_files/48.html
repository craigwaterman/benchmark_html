13 Habits of Frugal People

January 6, 2016January 11, 2016 Kayli

It can be tempting to give in to spur of the moment sales, or peer pressure to buy what is on trend for the season, but splurging too many times can become a lifestyle that leads to overspending and debt. Being frugal is an important piece of financial stability  and long term savings.

I grew up in a very frugal household. My parents did not splurge for cable, name brands, or even air conditioning. We braved the summers with fans, we watched our handful of television stations via antenna, and shopped at Walmart or Kmart most of the time. They never bought brand new cars or the newest technology. We used the same ragged towels for years, because they still worked despite looking worn down and torn.

Now that they are retired and all the kids are out of the house, my parents have learned to splurge more on themselves. They have cable, air-conditioning, new towels, and have moved to a newer (yet much smaller) condo. There is a time and a way to splurge the right way.

Growing up this way was certainly frustrating as a kid, but it has taught me many valuable lessons on saving money and being frugal. My husband and I don’t quite take our frugality to the level of my parents, but that is mostly because we don’t have children and so can splurge a little more than they could. Many of our habits seem like common sense, but I realize that many people were brought up in a variety of environments where they perhaps did not learn how to budget or make thrifty decisions.

So to help those individuals who may be wondering how to reduce their spending, here are a few habits of frugal people:

1) If you can’t pay for something in cash, don’t buy it. Don’t take out loans, or plan to just make payments on your credit card. This is one way people slowly end up in debt, because they just pay the minimum on their loans and credit cards.

2) Look for true sales. Not all sales are really worth while, so make sure what you’re buying is actually a good deal. Compare prices online if you can.

3) Don’t buy something you don’t need just because it’s on sale. If you don’t use it, it’s not really a good deal, is it? This is one way people end up with a lot of clutter because they buy all of this stuff they don’t need and don’t use.

4) Do your research before making a large purchase. Compare pricing, look at reviews, see when this item is most likely to go on sale, or look for a slightly used version on Ebay/Amazon/Craigslist.

5) Don’t pay more just out of convenience. Vending machines and checkout lines at stores are the worst. Everything is so expensive and in smaller packages. Precut vegetables and fruits are also marked up. If you can, buy things in their bigger/original form and break them down into smaller portions at home.  Ordering food for delivery? you’re going to have to tip the delivery person and many restaurants have a delivery surcharge. If you can, go pick it up yourself and save a few dollars.

6) Don’t let things go to waste. Use what you have in your fridge or pantry before going on a big shopping trip. This helps you avoid rotten or moldy food, and also keeps things less cluttered.  If you can’t use it in time, freeze it!  Most things can either be frozen as is, or can be cooked then frozen. Same idea goes for anything else in the house like clothes or household materials; if you aren’t going to use it, don’t just throw it away, donate it to Goodwill or give it to family/friends.  Still have a little bit of lotion at the bottom of the bottle? You’ll be surprised how much is actually left at the bottom if you take the time to empty it out, instead of throwing it away.

7) Don’t let peer pressure tempt you to buy designer brands or fancy decorations. I rarely ever buy any designer brands, and I don’t pay much for decorations or other household goods. I realized that no one knows where your clothes or decorations come from. Unless you tell them, they have no idea if you are wearing a big name designer, an average name brand item, or something your found on sale at say.. T.J.Maxx.  I love shopping at stores like Marshall’s and Ross, because some of the items are name brand and some are not – I don’t care as long as they fit well and look good.

8) Stop paying for services you don’t use. If you no longer go to the gym, terminate your membership. If you aren’t using Netflix, or you notice you don’t watch those expensive cable channels you are paying extra for, shut it down! You could ditch cable/satellite tv totally and just watch basic channels through an antenna. Have a video game subscription (think Xbox) but never use it anymore? Have a tanning salon membership, but you don’t use it?

9) Figure out your priorities and stick to them! It’s important to us to have solid retirement funds set, and to also have generous savings in our bank accounts in case of emergencies. I tend to shy away from splurging on things that are temporary. I don’t get my nails done, rarely every do I splurge for a pedicure, I keep my haircuts pretty simple so they cost less, and stopped highlighting my hair years ago. For me, it’s not worth it to spend a bunch of money on something temporary, even though it gives you great short-term satisfaction.

10) Use your credit cards strategically. Many cards now give you points toward gift cards or travel, which can save you a ton of money in the future. Look closely at how you earn points so that you know which card to use when. My husband has a card that gives him double or triple points depending on what he is purchasing, and we are planning to use these points to pay for our next vacation.

11) If you travel frequently, make sure you are signed up for each airline’s and hotel’s points program. Even rental car companies have points programs now. These points can really accumulate and in the end you might end up with a free vacation! My husband travels for work, so he racks up those hotel and airline points, which has helped us pay for pretty much every vacation we have taken.

12) Don’t make dining out a habit. We don’t eat out very often. Usually it’s only when we want to meet friends out for dinner on an occasional weekend, or we are just craving something that we can’t make at home. We could go weeks without eating out, and that’s a good thing for our budget. We always end up spending more than we anticipate at a restaurant; you get caught up in the food and drinks, and forget about the cost.  Instead, have a variety of food staples at home so that you can always whip up something for lunch or dinner, and pack your lunch for work.

13) Don’t buy the biggest house or fanciest car just because you can technically afford it. Buying an expensive car or home leaves you with large monthly payments, does it leave you extra money to put into your savings? Does it leave you room for an emergency? What about that nice vacation you were planning on next year, do you now have money for that? Don’t buy something that is going to leave you living pay check to pay check; always spend less than you think you should.
