HOW TO MANAGE YOUR ONLINE REPUTATION AND BUILD YOUR PERSONAL BRAND

Dan Macharia — June 18, 2015

Reputation management is a big deal for people in the limelight. Celebrities, politicians, business leaders all invest a lot of money to manage their reputations in the eyes of the masses. It’s obvious why they do this. They understand that they are only as good as their name. If your name is muddied by controversy and negative connotations, it’s hard to sell people your ideas, image or skills and be taken seriously.

For those of us who are not in the top echelons of society, we only have to manage our reputations in the eyes of a very limited circle of people, namely our friends, family, workmates, neighbors and at the very most acquaintances. This used to be the case, anyway. Today, you have as much a task of managing your reputation as those in the top most stratum of society. Why? Because we are now living in an information age where complete strangers meet you for the first time and the first thing they do is Google your name.

They don’t wait to sit down with you for a coffee and ask you about yourself. They reach for their smartphone or tablet and immediately run a Google search of you, sometimes even as they speak to you. If you have published or been published on anything on the Internet, you will pop up in a result. And the result that pops up is the reputation they will assume about you, not what you tell them.

Your reputation online is built using the building blocks that are every blog you’ve written, every status update you sent, every photo you uploaded, every profile you built out, every website you signed up to, among a dozen other things people do online. If you’ve attached your name to it, it is part of your online reputation and what picture it draws is of huge significance.

One story and a report demonstrate this very well. The CEO of Klout, the world’s largest online reputation platform once decided to tweak his platform’s algorithm and the result was that Klout member scores went down significantly. He initially thought that it would be no big deal and it would blow over. He was in for a rude shock. Within hours of scores changing, a Twitter campaign had already been set up under the OccupyKlout hash-tag. Shortly after, his phone number leaked and he went on to receive hundreds of death threats over the debacle.

In a report on the effect of Yelp scores on a restaurant, it was found that each star had an effect of between 5% and 9% on the revenues of a restaurant. This meant that every star you either got or lost as a restaurant in your ratings either added or lost you the equivalent to those percentages in your revenue.

These two illustrations demonstrate the huge importance of online reputation. It goes even further on online skill marketplaces such as Odesk and Elance where your reputation (or rating) means the difference between getting jobs and not.

If reputation management is so important, how do we build it up and ensure all that shows up online about us serves to only build our personal brand and not tear it down? It takes a very deliberate approach to what you do and say online.

Social Media

Social media is perhaps the single biggest reputation management platform online today. It’s so pervasive that when someone Googles your name, the first results to show up are almost always your social media profiles. So if a potential employer searched your name and lands on your social media profiles, would you be at ease with them sifting through your status updates and photos? If they visit your LinkedIn profile, would they see a glowing resume of someone primed to be hired? Here are some examples of social media instances where your activities degrade your online reputation significantly:

An incomplete LinkedIn profile: There’s probably nothing worse than this especially if you consider yourself a professional. Profiles that lack a good image, a well itemized list of experience and other credentials scream incompetence. They show a potential employer that you lack the seriousness to even maintain a complete LinkedIn profile, so can they trust you with bigger things? My advice: If you cannot maintain a fully completed LinkedIn profile, delete your account and do without it. You’re much better off this way.

Facebook, Twitter and Google Plus profiles full of trivial things: I know those Internet videos and Vines are hilarious and those memes just seem to get the joke at its funniest place but resist the urge. As an aspiring business/ professional leader and someone keen to build a credible and respectable personal brand this is probably not something you should engage in wholesale. The option of this is to keep your profile completely private but this may become a liability if one day your boss sends you a friend/follow invite. The best way to manage these social media profiles is to be always aware of the message they send out about you. If you wouldn’t want your boss or business partner to read a status update, perhaps consider not putting it up in the first place.

Negative Blogs: If you have a blog, fantastic. If it’s a blog where you rant and rave about the unfairness of life and how your boss lacks leadership skills, not so great. Keeping a blog is perhaps the most powerful form of reputation management because you get a chance to think about what you want to say before publishing. Penning intelligent and thoughtful articles and blog posts can be a huge boost to your reputation and personal brand so do have a blog, only be careful what you write.

The challenge of reputation management perhaps hits the hardest when it comes to young, tech savvy professionals. The ones who are very active on the Internet across multiple platforms. That sense of participation is great but you should be careful if you fall in this bracket because as the saying goes, the Internet never forgets. Be careful what photos you post, comments you make, etc. Be always sure you have nothing that will come back and bite you when you are interviewing for that senior associate position.

Your personal brand in the online age is all you have these days. It can make you or unmake you. It will follow you wherever you go, whether in business or employment so take time to invest in building it. Make sure your online reputation is working hard to build your personal brand exactly how you want people to perceive and consume it if and when they do Google you.
