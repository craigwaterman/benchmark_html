The Most Common Branding Mistakes to avoid

  • Posted on June 19, 2014
  • by mayart
  • in Uncategorized
  • 27 Comments

Name a soda company. Name a sportswear company. Name a food provider. What companies did you name? Why did those companies pop first in your head? The reason why you chose the companies that you did was because of successful marketing. Branding is all about conveying a message through advertisement, logos, and your product. It is about establishing not only a level of quality, but a positive association that will bring people back again and again.

There is a lot to be gained through branding, but there is also a lot to lose. Successful branding is a challenge many companies invest resources in because it can be difficult to create that perfect brand image. Lets take a look at the most common branding mistakes, and examine each category to see where they have gone wrong, and where you can go right.

1. The Visual Message

Visuals are everything. From the double arches of McDonalds to the check sign of Nike, establishing a visual brand is as important as it is complex. Simply put, your customer must hear and see your name, logo, tag line and colors consistently over and over and over in many different ways before you are imprinted on your customer’s mind map. Lets take a look at a few ways you can help your brand accomplish this goal.

Problem with Visuals

So much of successful branding comes from word and image association. When a person is thinking about buying a product or service you offer, the first thing that should come to their mind is the name of your business. This is where successful branding comes in. Whether it is your logo, or any other visual you associate with your brand, it should speak to what you do as a company along with your ability to provide the service effectively. This requires branding communication to get the best possible sense of what works.

Problems with Tag Lines

Poor branding messages often include ineffective, inappropriate, or just plane wrong tag lines. Remember, this is not what you consider to be right or wrong, but what the customer considers right or wrong concerning your business. Branding mistakes include making the tag line have to do with lofty goals that do not directly reflect the company in some way. “Striving for Excellence,” for example, can be used by an auto dealership, investment bank, or any number of other company types. In addition, how the tag line is visually displayed and centered may create an unappealing logo.

As an additional note, some people consider it a failure if the tag line lacks a call to action. A call to action is designed to hook the customer and give them reason to come in. The can take many forms. However, carefully choosing the right call to action is critical. Not only must it reflect your business and direction, but also fit in visually with the rest of the image you are creating. In the eyes of the customer, your brand must be seamless.

Stale Content

A major cause of branding mistakes comes from having stale content relating to your branding message. Where as some things remain timeless, human interest and needs tend to change. Even when it comes to products that everyone needs regardless of technology (like toilet paper,) the ways in which the product is advertised successful does change. In addition, visual branding can become outdated leading people to choose more up to date designs under the belief that the company behind the logo and brand is somehow better. Stale content can be solved through branding communication, working with groups that specialize in graphic design and content, and understand effective ways to market your product.

2. Forgetting the Customer

In the most elementary terms, every business must have a customer. These customers often represent a particular demographic. Businesses make it a policy to market towards their demographic, but sometimes forget to do this when branding themselves and their products. As a result, successful branding comes from being able to strike an agreement between all competing interests.

For example, lets take a look at the logo, or any other image created to represent the company. Besides the input of the customers, a business will also be considering other factors including investor interest, their ranking compared to similar businesses, and internal opinions. As a result, a visual that works perfectly with one group may not work across the board, leading to the customer being forgotten through improper visual branding. By spending more time on visual branding and the consumer, this problem can be solved.

As a final note, beware the trap of trying to appease everyone. While throwing your net as far as possible may at first seem wise, most companies have better luck knowing whom to target. The more specific you define your demographic, the more precise your branding can be.

3. Self-Determination, Branding, and A Visual Message

One of the biggest branding mistakes is not having an actualized model of what you or your company are. This may at first seem counterintuitive. I mean, how can you have a business without knowing what your business is about? What it all comes down to is having a precise understanding of the who, what, where, why, and how of your brand.

The perfect tag lines, logo, and colors can bring the complexity of your company together. As a result, many companies focus on graphic design to unify their message and actualize their goals. A great benefit also comes from knowing the services you provide backwards and forwards.

A crucial mistake many companies make is not remembering that self-actualization is a two-way streak. As you strive to define your business, your customers are working on their impression of you. This impression can make or break your products and business model. To get an idea of what other people are thinking about your brand, know what your customers think. Successful branding requires branding communication with your customers to be able to deliver the most straightforward and clear understanding of what your brand is all about. More often then not, this involves having a clear, understandable logo and graphic that quickly conveys how important your business is.

As an exercise, try summing up your brand in 10 words or less. Try to picture your company as a single object. By spending the time to fully flesh out your brand, you actualize your model and bring an element of pro-profit self-determination into the business world.

4. Buzzwords

Buzzwords are an industry wide problem. Buzzwords are used to relate a relatively complex idea through a single word that encapsulates an idea, emotion, call to action, solution, and resolution. They often come up as a part of a major marketing trend. However, it is argued that buzzwords are infrequently used correctly in business. Buzzwords have a tendency to become outdated and even laughable relatively quickly as well. If you disagree, then take a look at “shifting paradigms,” “thinking outside the box,” “big data,” or even “slow foods.” What do these things really mean? Will your customers know what this means?

When considering successful branding, customers like it when your company and your brand relay the message of competence. Buzz words can help that message, as some customers may be aware of the word and impressed by its usage, but it can also hurt by obfuscating your true purpose and seeming outdate. Once again, branding communication is key. Test and see what works when it comes to buzzwords. Test to your demographic and plan accordingly.

Similar to a buzz-word, your logo, and other visuals that represent your company will be the main way you communicate with customers. Will they understand what your brand means and what it can do for them? Or, will your customer not really know what it means? By putting time and effort into graphic design, you can deliver again and again a consistent message to your customer.

5. Little Attention To Employees or Training

In many corporate cultures, branding is seen as a necessary evil worth only the minimal amount of time, attention and money. Often enough, this leads to departments being understaffed and underutilized. This can turn into a long-term problem for the company.

One common solution to this problem, besides proper staffing and finances, is to focus on each employee. Consider each employee a mascot for the company. Whether they are wearing a suit or on vacation, they will invariably be talking about the company they work for. Work to build employee loyalty, improve the conditions in work, and make the brand worth something to the employees. With a little extra time and money, mistakes, omissions, and ignorance regarding the principles behind the branding can be minimized.

As you can clearly see, a lot goes into successful branding. However, by stressing your name, logo, tag line and colors consistently again and again, you can imprint on your customers mind a business worth investing money in.
