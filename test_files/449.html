Finding Third-Party Safety Resources


From the July-August 2005 issue of Public Power

Originally published

Safety
By Bill Atkinson
freelance writer

Regardless of how large or small your utility is, it’s unlikely that you have all of the safety and health expertise that you need available in-house. Even a giant like New York Power Authority relies on third-party safety and health experts to complement its in-house expertise. Third-party services are available through private consultants, broker and carrier loss control professionals, cooperative arrangements and state Occupational Safety and Health Administration consulting services.

Dyersburg Electric System in Tennessee identifies consultants by communicating with other utilities for recommendations on safety trainers. “We use a private consultant who comes in and does substance abuse safety training,” said Patsy Roark, vice president of human resources for Dyersburg Electric. “She was referred to us by another utility and she has done an excellent job for us.”

New York Power Authority has multi-year contracts with consultants for industrial hygiene, safety and occupational health services. Every three or four years, the utility either renews these or seeks new consultants. NYPA is required to publish a formal request for proposals in the New York State Contract Reporter, said Ed Smith, senior industrial hygiene/occupational health specialist. “If we hear about a particular consultant through talking with other utilities, we may add that consultant’s name to the bidder’s list.” NYPA selects its consultants based on the criteria of the “lowest qualified bidder,” with the emphasis on the word “qualified.” It is important to create comprehensive parameters in the request for proposals, such as making sure the consultants have the appropriate credentials and skills. “In addition, we have criteria for time of response in the event of an emergency, such as being able to respond within three or four hours, if necessary,” said Smith.

The Northwest Public Power Association provides safety training to its member utilities through a private third-party utility safety consulting firm. “We selected this company based on its expertise,” said Ron Marley, training manager. Its consultants have done field work in the utility industry, which gives them credibility with NWPPA’s members’ employees. “In addition, they have strong safety backgrounds and the ability to train,” he said. The company has consultants in various regions of the Northwest who are available 24/7 to NWPPA members. Each has a specialty and travels to member utilities to provide training and advice on their particular areas of expertise, such as grounding.

Generally, NWPPA members have been pleased with the consultants. “However, some of our members are interested in different levels of safety services,”  said Marley. “As a result, we are investigating the possibility of providing our members with additional safety training options.”

Loss control specialists available through an insurance broker or carrier offer a less expensive alternative. Eugene Water and Electric Board in Oregon has used loss control services provided by the utility’s workers’ comp carrier and insurance broker, said EWEB Safety Coordinator Doug Caven. These experts have worked with the utility on industrial hygiene issues.”

NYPA also relies on these specialists. “We use them as information resources,” said Noel DesChamps, director of power generation support services for NYPA. Insurance company personnel occasionally attend NYPA safety committee meetings to brief utility staff on fire inspection activities and provide advice and information on accident prevention.

While Dyersburg uses a private consultant for part of its safety and health initiative, it relies primarily on cooperative initiatives with other utilities. “There is a job training and safety program set up by the state for utilities, based in Murfreesboro, Tenn.,” said Roark. The program has three trainers, one each for the eastern, middle and western parts of the state. Each month, they schedule a 90-minute training program covering a wide variety of safety topics, such as pole-top bucket truck rescue, hazard communication, fire safety, etc. “We also participate in the state Department of Transportation’s work zone safety training program, which covers flagging training, herbicide spraying training, and other topics,” she said. Finally, Dyersburg uses the services of the Tennessee Valley Public Power Association for its apprenticeship training, which includes correspondence courses and three- to five-day off-site labs that its employees attend.

The federal Occupational Safety and Health Administration offers consulting services to private-sector employers. “Since federal OSHA does not have authority over public sector employers, we are not permitted to provide consulting services to public sector employers,” said Paula White, who heads the directorate of cooperative and state programs for OSHA. “However, state OSHA organizations are permitted to provide these services to public sector employers.” Some state OSHAs provide consulting services only to public sector employers, she said. The states and territories that offer OSHA consultation services to public sector employers are: Alaska, Arizona, California, Connecticut, Hawaii, Indiana, Iowa, Kentucky, Maryland, Michigan, Minnesota, Nevada, New Jersey, New Mexico, New York, North Carolina, Oregon, Puerto Rico, South Carolina, Tennessee, Utah, Vermont, Virgin Islands, Virginia, Washington, and Wyoming.

The New York Power Authority takes advantage of state-sponsored safety and industrial hygiene services. “Our regulator for occupational health and safety is the New York State Public Employee Safety and Health Bureau,” said Smith. “The bureau has a consultation branch, which NYPA has turned to for assistance and training on job hazard analysis, accident investigation, ergonomics and electrical safety training.”

In 1999, Eugene Water & Electric Board asked the Oregon OSHA to help with an assessment of safety training needs. “They let us know where we stood and where we could make improvements,” said Caven. He met with two Oregon OSHA consultants, who toured EWEB facilities and completed a hazard survey. OSHA representatives identified about 100 items that needed to be fixed. “They let us know which ones were serious and which weren’t, then gave us time to address them,” he said. The OSHA consultants then asked the utility if it wanted to pursue the SHARP designation (Safety & Health Achievement Recognition Program), and management agreed. At the time, the utility rated about 50 percent of what the standards were for the SHARP program. Management realized it needed to make improvements.

“We created operational performance measurements for safety and hazard mitigation,” said Caven. “We made a commitment to not only address issues that the OSHA inspectors identified, but additional ones that our safety committee identified.” The utility also brought in an industrial hygienist who looked at respiratory protection, chemical inventories and material safety data sheets. The three-pronged assessment resulted in 250 recommendations for improvement.

Management committed to making sure the issues were addressed. “We also re-energized our safety committee and got a lot more employee participation in general,” said Caven. Over the next four years, the utility completed all improvements. OSHA did a second evaluation and, in late 2004, the utility received the SHARP Award. Results went beyond just the award, though. The utility cut its recordable accident rate to 32 last year from an average of 37, and it cut lost-time days to 188 from an average of 398.

Throughout the process, Oregon OSHA was very helpful whenever the utility sought advice, Caven said. During consultation, utilities are free from general enforcement activities, he said. This was confirmed when a compliance officer came in and wanted to conduct a general audit. “I told him we were in consultation,” said Caven. “He made a quick call and confirmed it, then went on his way.” However, being in consultation won’t prevent you from getting a citation if a compliance officer happens to be around and witnesses an unsafe act or condition or other violation of OSHA rules.
