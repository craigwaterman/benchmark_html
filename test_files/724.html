Science and Weight Control

May 1, 2015 By Michael Wood

Weight control is one of the toughest things a person can face. If you have conquered weight loss and successfully obtained your ideal weight, then you have already faced a difficult challenge. You must now prepare for a lifetime of controlling your weight and maintaining a healthy lifestyle so that your previous efforts will not be made in vain. Thanks to various studies, there are numerous things that science can teach us about weight control. Keeping these in mind throughout the process can help you maintain a healthy weight and ultimate lead to a healthier lifestyle.

  • Weight control requires regular maintenance
  • Protein and carbohydrates must be monitored
  • Psychological factors affect weight control
  • Weight loss method does not affect weight control results
  • Self-monitoring is a key to successful maintenance
  • Medical issues can affect weight control

Weight control requires regular maintenance:

Just because you lost weight does not mean that you are home free. Maintaining your weight can be almost if not more difficult of a challenge as losing the weight in the first place. Studies have shown that those who have lost weight actually increase their calorie intake and reduce their energy expenditure once they lose their desired weight. The result from a 1999 study published in the Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology showed that 35% of those who lost weight regained the weight due to maintenance issues. This means that successful weight control must involve regular maintenance such as continuing a healthy diet and exercising regularly.

Diet and exercise are the two most important factors in weight control. While diet has been shown to outweigh the importance of exercise, physical activity is still something that must be part of regular weight control. In addition, physical activity and healthy diet are things that must be maintained throughout one’s entire life in order to ensure that the weight stays off. However, another study also reported that weight control measures become easier over time for those who have maintained a steady weight.


Even though more emphasis is often placed on diet rather than exercise, it has been shown that physical activities can help increase your metabolism, allowing your body to burn through the fat you consume. If you have a healthy metabolism rate, weight control becomes easier.

Protein and carbohydrates must be monitored:

Just as important as calorie and fat intake is the consumption of foods with protein and carbohydrates. Choosing the right combination of both is essential in maintaining a desired weight. Too much protein can cause your body to burn the excess as calories, thereby leaving fat and carbohydrates being unprocessed by your body (resulting in the storage of fat). Too little can cause your body to take protein from where you don’t want it to, such as breaking down your muscles in order to use the amount it needs. Carbohydrates can cause similar issues such as turning into fat which gets store in your body. Under-consumption of carbohydrates can cause you to not have enough energy in your body to get you through physical activities such as that of exercising. A healthy balance between the intake of both carbohydrates and protein is essential for having energy to exercise and the nutrients needed to maintain a healthy body.

To maintain a healthy weight, it is recommended that you consume approximately 50-55% of daily calories by way of carbohydrates, 10-15% from protein, and the remainder from fat. If you are losing weight, the ratios change to 20-50% carbohydrates, 15% from protein, and the remainder in fat. Of course these numbers should be adjusted based on your doctor’s recommendations.

Psychological factors affect weight control:

According to another study published in the Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology, psychological factors can affect weight control. The study was conducted with individuals who had already achieved long-term weight loss. The results were 59% that maintained control of their weight with the remainder actually gaining weight. Factors such as depression, dietary disinhibition, and binge eating were attributed with those gaining weight.

A 2009 study by the University of Alabama showed that people who are depressed gain weight on a faster pace than those who aren’t. This shows that depression is linked to weight gain and will affect weight control. Those trying to control weight without getting help with depression are less likely to keep the weight off. While depression does not cause weight gain, it does increase factors such as binge eating which can directly result in increased weight.

Weight loss method does not affect weight control results:

Most people wanting to lose a large amount of weight go by way of the surgical route. As such, there have been very few studies as of late that compare non-surgical weight loss and surgical weight loss in relation to each one’s weight control. The National Weight Control Registry (NWCR) is one of the only to provide resources for comparing such data and in a 2009 study it was found that weight control success was not significant in one group or the other (surgical vs. non-surgical). The behavioral differences between the subjects of the study were significant, as was the way they initially lost the weight. However, once the weight was off, the issues related to the maintenance of weight came down to factors other than the method of original weight loss.

Self- monitoring is a key to successful maintenance:

Self-monitoring is the process of keeping track of your own weight control with regular weighing, documenting of food intake, and review of weight control techniques and their results. Technology has become very important in self-monitoring with the use of electronic scales, Internet resources, and online personal assistants. A review of studies published between 1993 and 2009 found that there is a strong relationship between self-monitoring and successful weight control. This tells us that self-monitoring must be added to weight control maintenance in order to maintain a healthy weight.

Part of regular maintenance should include self-weighing. Those who weigh themselves are more likely to keep control of their weight due to catching slight upticks in weight before it becomes too much to handle. Immediate gratification is another reason why self-monitoring works. Instead of waiting to go to the doctor to see results, progress can be tracked instantly from reviewing your actual weight on a scale or using online tools to track progress.

Medical issues can affect weight control:

Medical issues can cause all sorts of problems for those trying to maintain weight and you don’t have to travel far to see examples. For anyone trying to maintain a desired weight, diagnosis of a medical problem could force you to change your weight control method. Medical issues have also been found to have a direct relation to weight control.

Cushing’s syndrome is an issue that can throw weight control programs out of sync. Cushing’s is when your adrenal glands produce too much cortisol. The result is the buildup of fat, mostly concentrated in the upper back, stomach, and face. Someone with undiagnosed Cushing’s could feel like their weight control program is not effective. Another issue is hypothyroidism, a problem caused by the underproduction of thyroid which results in a lower metabolism rate. A lower metabolism means that your body cannot burn through fat as well and ultimately leads to weight gain.
