Exercise That Counts

by wrbc on Aug.14, 2010, under Uncategorized

As a middle aged man, I have recently started taking my health more seriously, especially since having heart by-pass surgery several months ago.  Almost every weekday morning, I am on the treadmill, the stationary bicycle, the universal weight machine, and in the pool, exercising vigorously to try to keep my body in shape.  Now I hate exercise, but I have learned that I need to discipline myself for the purpose of maintaining my health.

One day it dawned on me that I was spending more time on the treadmill each day than I was on my knees in prayer—and that’s something of an indictment for a pastor.  The words that came to mind were those of the Apostle Paul to Timothy, found in 1 Timothy 4:7-9:  “On the other hand, discipline yourself for the purpose of godliness; for bodily discipline is only of little profit, but godliness is profitable for all things, since it holds promise for the present life and also for the life to come.  It is a trustworthy statement deserving full acceptance.”

That last verse is revealing, isn’t it?  This is a trustworthy statement deserving complete acceptance.  Obviously, it was not something about which everybody was convinced!  Paul had warned against being preoccupied with worldly fables only fit for old women.  Apparently there was in 1st-century Ephesus, some sort of counterpart to today’s soap operas and romance novels.  Stay away from that garbage, says Paul.  Commit yourself to personal discipline.  Perhaps thinking of the rigorous training required of an Olympic athlete, Paul then commends the virtue of self-discipline to his associate, but not the discipline of physical training.  Rather, he is concerned for the rigorous application of spiritual self-discipline.  This, according to the Apostle, is a much more valuable discipline.

Why?  Because working out physically is good for the body, but the body is temporal.  We will all eventually die, regardless of how much we exercise.  Our souls, however, continue in conscious existence, something concerning which Paul reminds the Corinthians when he observes that “while we are at home in the body we are absent from the Lord” (2 Cor 5:6).  To be absent from the body, therefore, is to be present with the Lord.  As one little poem reminds us, “Only one life, ‘twill soon be past; only what’s done for Christ will last.”

In Timothy’s case, Paul explains what is entailed in this self-discipline:  it meant living a blameless life “in speech, conduct, love, faith and purity,” showing himself to be an example of what it means to be a believer (v. 12).  It meant giving ample attention to the Word of God so that he could read it, teach it, and exhort others from it (v. 13).  And it meant exercising his spiritual giftedness in ministry to others (v. 14).  A quality moral and ethical life, a mastery of the Scriptures, and a diligent stewardship of the abilities God had given for ministry.

But this kind of life doesn’t just happen, and Paul goes on to tell Timothy to “take pains with these things; be absorbed in them, so that your progress will be evident to all.  Pay close attention to yourself and your teaching; persevere in these things” (vv. 15-16a).  Why?  The reason could not be more important:  “for as you do this you will ensure salvation both for yourself and for those who hear you.”  This exercise is not only for your sake, but for the sake of many others as well.

Ask yourself, then, am I spending more time exercising than I am reading God’s Word and praying?  Maybe it’s time for a little adjustment so that you get more of the exercise that really counts!
