Financial Literacy Month: Common terms to know

Apr 10

Posted by cambridgesavingsblog

April is Financial Literacy Month, and Cambridge Savings Bank wants to make sure you know your stuff. We put together a list of terms to help you brush up on essential financial terms.

Credit – an amount of money available to a borrower, which must be repaid on time, with interest

Line of Credit – a predetermined amount of credit available to a borrower from a lender

Debt – the total amount of money a borrower owes to lenders

Interest – a fee owed to a lender by a borrower

Interest bearing account – any account that earns interest on money deposited in the account

Principal balance –the base amount of money owed to a lender without the addition of any interest

Rate of Return – the percentage rate of interest an account will generate over a particular period of time, either annually or over the lifetime of the account

Asset – any item or account with monetary value

Bankruptcy – the legal process for an individual or business who is unable to pay back outstanding debts

Liquidation – the process of converting assets into cash, typically during bankruptcy

Secured Loan – a loan or line of credit given with an agreement that another item of value, known as collateral, will be forfeited to the lender if the borrower fails to repay the loan (for example, if you get a loan to purchase a car, the car is typically used to secure the loan and the lender can repossess the car if you don’t make your payments)

Mortgage – a secured debt where the borrower receives a loan to purchase property

Foreclosure – the process where a lender takes possession of property as repayment when a borrower can’t make payment on a loan

Unsecured Loan – a loan without collateral, that charges higher fees and interest rates

Lien –legal claim against property to secure payment of a debt

Equity – the monetary value of a property or business after repayment of a loan (for example, your home is worth $300,000, but you owe $200,000 on your mortgage, then your home equity is $100,000)

For more financial terms, you can check out last year’s list: 18 Financial Terms to Know

5 tips for winning at fantasy football and personal finance

Sep 13

Posted by cambridgesavingsblog

Football season is upon us. Many of us are involved in a fantasy football league (or ten). You may have never thought about it, but you can employ many of the strategies you use in fantasy football to your personal finances. Here are five ways to win at both.

  1. Do your research – Serious fantasy football players plan out a draft strategy weeks before they participate in their draft. They spend days analyzing commentary in blogs, preseason performance, statistics and rankings. They strategize, keeping in mind that their picks during the draft will affect their success for the rest of the season. Are you expending as much energy understanding your investments, analyzing your budget and planning for your retirement, as you are on your draft? It is crucial to do your research and be as prepared as you can when making financial decisions. Spending money often presents the same challenges. Be sure to do enough research to know all of your available options and where you can get the best deals.
  2. Have a contingency plan – Nobody can predict the future. Even if you have done your research, and are first place in your league after three weeks, things can happen. You probably have a backup plan if two of your star players end up on the injured list for the rest of the season. The same is true when managing your money. Unexpected expenses come up frequently, so make sure you are prepared by establishing an emergency fund.
  3. Monitor and adjust – In fantasy football, people stay abreast of who is getting the most receptions and touchdowns, who is injured, and who has been having a few bad weeks. Think of this like saving for your retirement. Your initial plan isn’t set in stone. You are much more likely to have enough money through your retirement years if you keep up to date on what’s happening with your investments and with the economy in general. Just like you can make trades or substitutions in fantasy football, you can make educated changes to your retirement plan as well.
  4. Budgeting for the most valuable positions – Many fantasy leagues have a salary cap or bid system for draft picks. You may have $50 million for your entire roster but spend $25 million to be sure Adrian Peterson is on your team. Even if your league isn’t set up like this, drafting a kicker in the first round probably isn’t the best use of a pick. Budgeting works the same way. It is important to identify your most important expenses first and be sure that you have enough money to cover them. For example, the biggest portion of your budget may go towards your mortgage or rent, and you should avoid spending on things that aren’t as necessary until you cover that expense.
  5. Defense wins championships – Have you ever lost a game on Sunday because the Bears scored a defensive touchdown, had three sacks, a handful of fumble recoveries and an interception? There may have been some great offensive plays, but the defense was able to stop everything that was thrown their way. Playing good defense with your personal finances means making regimented and educated decisions, rather than impulsive or emotional ones.

As you watch football this season and manage your fantasy team, remember that you can apply some of the same principles to managing your money. This season, maybe you’ll win at both!
