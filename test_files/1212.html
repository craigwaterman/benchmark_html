And Other Storeys

If you are going to write a novel pretty much entirely set inside an apartment block then you are going to have to accept that people are going to compare it to Life, A User's Manual. Unless, of course, they haven't read Life, A User's Manual, in which case they probably won't bother comparing anything against it. But I have read Life, A User's Manual and it is one of my favourite books ever, so I am going to compare all I want and there's nothing Bradley Somer, or you, frankly, can do about it.

For the uninitiated—and fucking well sort it out if that means you—Life, A User's Manual is a novel by French author Georges Perec, the events of which all take place within a few seconds inside a Paris apartment block. Perec famously plotted his novel by sketching out a grid representing the various apartments and then moving a knight chess piece around the grid, visiting every square just once. Although he deliberately left one square out because he loved shit like that. Somehow he managed to capture the events in every room, plus everyone's backstory, in a beautiful mess of a novel.

In Fishbowl, Bradley Somer tries something very similar but throws in some classic TV drama tropes and a bit of extra whimsy for good measure. Life, A User's Manual it ain't (there's my comparison for you), but it is a bloody good read.

The novel is book-ended by Ian. Ian is a goldfish. In a foolish moment, Ian decides to leap out of his goldfish bowl. This is particularly foolish as, today, Ian's bowl is on the balcony of his owner's apartment. The apartment is on the 27th floor. Ian is out on the balcony because he was looking at Connor, his owner, in a funny way when Connor was fucking Faye, who most definitely is not his girlfriend, and it was putting Connor off his stroke. Hence his relegation to the balcony.

Many of the events in the novel are briefly witnessed by Ian as he plummets to the sidewalk below. It is a silly conceit, and fun, but, actually, a tad superfluous as Somer brilliantly splits and paces the stories of his many characters without needing to resort to a voyeuristic terminal velocity goldfish.

So we have Connor frantically throwing his mistress out of the door when his girlfriend buzzes to be let up. Petunia going into labour just as her phone runs out of battery and the elevator breaks down. Jimenez the handyman setting fire to said elevator while attempting to fix it. Garth, the construction worker, returning home with a mystery parcel, the contents of which will reveal his deepest secret. Claire, the agoraphobic sex-line operator baking a quiche. Young Herman coming home to find his grandfather, his only guardian, has died and now he aimlessly wanders the corridors.

These, and a host of peripheral characters, go about their business, their storylines chopped into small pieces and jumbled around a bit so that the reader is given tantalising glimpses for most of the novel's length before they all start to come together towards the end.

It is an expertly judged bit of writing. Brilliantly paced. Funny and, occasionally, very moving. Somer has a great eye for detail and an even better eye for the way people act.

I liked it a lot. Can you tell?

Fishbowl is published next month by those nice people at Ebury.

June: In Review

Another June has gone by and I read some books, so I did.

Delacorta - Diva

Diva was one of the World Cinema classics of the 1980s. I finally got round to watching a couple of months ago. It was good. Very good. A bit too arty and hip, perhaps, but with a cracking crime story underneath. Anyway, I ordered the book that inspired it and read it in more or less one sitting. It is also very good and differs from the film enough to keep you guessing when it comes to the plot. Delacorta was the pen name of Daniel Odier who wrote several more literary works under his real name as well as a series of thrillers under his pretend one.

Riccardo Orizio - Talk of the Devil

An Italian journalist attempts to meet and interview seven ex-dictators. A fascinating insight into what makes these nutjobs tick and also what life is like after power. So we find Idi Amin hanging around Saudi boxing gyms, Baby Doc Duvalier planning to return to Haiti and install solar panels all over the place, General Jaruzelski writing and self-publishing two volumes of memoir to put across his side of the story, and so on. Orizio is rather deadpan about the whole thing, pointing out the facts, and accusations, regarding each dictator, and often asking them direct questions about them, but never pontificating to the reader.

John Higgs - Stranger Than We Can Imagine: Making Sense of the Twentieth Century

This hugely entertaining alternative view of history re-examines the previous century from several interesting and unusual viewpoints and features a fascinating cast of characters such as Aleister Crowley, Baroness Elsa von Freytag-Loringhoven (who may actually have been the creator of Marcel Duchamp's infamous urinal art), Albert Einstein, Wernher von Braun, Shigeru Miyamoto (the man behind Super Mario Bros) and Marie Stopes. It isn't published till the end of August so I'll probably bang on about it a lot more then.

Ryan Gattis - All Involved

Set over the six days of the 1992 Los Angeles riots, with a series of characters narrating interlocking stories, this is a hugely impressive novel. Gattis captures the cadence and attitude of the Chicano gang members that feels authentic to this middle-aged, middle-class reader. A masterclass of both voice and storytelling.

Kimmy Waters - Uptalk

I loved this poetry collection from Bottlecap Press. Funny, romantic, moving, beautifully observed and will some real balls to it.

Clifton Robbins - Smash and Grab

I have been making my way through this series of 1930s crime novels featuring barrister-detective Clay Harrison. This is my fourth now. They are a tad far-fetched but lots of fun. This one involves a fake smash and grab raid, a bystander caught in crossfire who isn't what he first seems, a cross-dressing criminal and a trip to Bruges.

Marie Phillips - The Table of Less Valued Knights

Sir Humphrey isn't a good enough knight to sit at the Round Table with King Arthur, but that doesn't stop him going an a quest to help a damsel in distress, albeit only when she turns up once all the other knights have gone to bed. Phillips' long-awaited follow-up to Gods Behaving Badly is just as funny and pokes fun at our own expectations just as much as it does Arthurian legend.

Ben Johncock - The Last Pilot

I have watched the evolution of this book from a polite distance over many years and I am delighted to see it finally published. What surprised me, having not read the whole thing before now, is what an accomplished and assured debut it is. Set in the world of US test pilots and astronauts, and in the home life of one of them, it balances the scientific and the technical with the domestic most deftly.

So there you have it. I am having a bit of an internet break at the moment, both in terms of blog and social media, while I work on a few projects that demand my attention, so apologies if updates here are few and far between for a while.

Write Words: A Novel Retreat

I am happy to plug this writing course hosted by two mighty fine novelists. Well worth checking out if you have a work in progress or are thinking of getting your arse in gear with this writing lark.

 

Write Words: A Novel Retreat

November 20th–25th, 2015

Novelists Stephanie Butland and Shelley Harris are delighted to offer a residential writing course in the glorious setting of Gladstone’s Library, North Wales.

The five-night course, Write Words: a Novel Retreat, will run from Friday November 20th – Wednesday November 25th,  with workshops, one-to-one consultations and plenty of time to simply write.  

The course

Write Words is for anyone working on a novel, whether they’ve just begun or hit their stride.

Participate in workshops to kick-start your creative thinking alongside sessions to help you clarify the story you’re telling, get to know your characters, and self-edit in a way that will get your reader gripped.

Get specific in one-to-ones with both Stephanie and Shelley so they can offer bespoke guidance.

Spend mornings getting all you can from formal teaching, and after lunch you’re free to write – either in your room or in the stunning vaulted library. If you’d rather read, the lounge boasts leather sofas and a roaring open fire, and you’re free to borrow any book from the library’s shelves.

 The setting

This unique place (in Welsh border country, just twenty minutes’ drive from Chester) was founded by Prime Minister William Gladstone, who wondered ‘how to bring together readers who had no books and books who had no readers’. Gladstone always intended the library to be residential; today the award-winning accommodation is listed as a Sawdays Special Place. Alistair Sawday says of it: ‘Come for as long as you need to recover from this mad world. It will … feel like a privilege.’ In January, the library topped a Guardian readers’ poll of wellbeing retreats. 

The food is delicious, and wherever possible is locally-sourced, Fair Trade and organic. The course is full board: three meals a day and the all-important tea / coffee and biscuits. For those wanting something a little stronger, there’s an honesty bar in the Gladstone Room and wine can be bought with meals.

The cost

The course fee, which includes everything except travel to and from the Library, is £750 for a single ensuite room. If you’d like to come with a friend, there are a limited number of double and twin ensuite rooms, for which the cost is £635 per person.

Places are limited! They’ll be allocated on a first come, first served basis - if you want to write a novel, we want to help you. So book 20-25 November out in your diary and email us today!

Haruki Murakami and Kafka on the Shore

Last week the Ninagawa Company performed two plays in Japanese at the Barbican. One was Hamlet, the other was an adaptation of Haruki Murakami's novel Kafka on the Shore. I was honoured to be asked to write the programme notes for the latter. It was a remarkable production: breathtaking set design, strong performances throughout and, I thought, an excellent retelling of the novel.

I thought some of you might like to read what I wrote.

Talking cats, transgender librarians, truck drivers and Colonel Sanders may not seem the most obvious ingredients for an international bestseller but Kafka on the Shore is no ordinary story and Haruki Murakami is no ordinary author.

Born in Kyoto, Japan in 1949, the only son of a pair of teachers, Murakami grew up immersing himself in American culture; reading hard-boiled crime novels, listening to rock ‘n’ roll and, eventually, becoming a jazz enthusiast.

He met his wife, Yoko, at university and they set up a jazz bar together, the Peter Cat. It was, by all accounts, a popular and successful venue but something happened to Murakami in 1978 that changed the course of his life. While sitting in the stands during a baseball match, his favourite team Yakult Swallows versus Hiroshima Carps, he was suddenly struck with the idea of writing a novel. ‘It was like a revelation, something out of the blue,’ he said later. On the way home he bought pens and paper and starting writing what was to become Hear the Wind Sing. It was published in 1979 and won the Gunzō newcomers award. The jazz bar owner had become a novelist.

The books that followed became popular with Japanese youth during the early 1980s, his use of twenty-something narrators and colloquial language made his writing instantly accessible to a generation that often felt shut out of the mannered, restrained world of literary fiction. But he found an even wider audience when he published Norwegian Wood in 1987, a more traditional love story or, at least, as traditional as Murakami is likely to get. It became a phenomenon in Japan. Fans started dressing to match the book’s jackets (it came in two volumes: one red, the other green), a muzak cover version of the Beatles song went to #1 in the charts and there was even a Norwegian Wood air freshener on supermarket shelves.

Uncomfortable with this level of fame, Murakami left Japan to travel the world, eventually settling in America where he wrote the book most critics believe to be his masterpiece, The Wind-up Bird Chronicle. It was certainly the novel that propelled him into the upper echelons of world literature—it became his first global bestseller—and as he himself said: ‘I’m sure that my importance as a novelist would have been greatly affected if this book hadn’t appeared.’

Kafka on the Shore was published almost a decade after The Wind-up Bird Chronicle, by which time Murakami was back in Japan, and marked somewhat of a departure. Gone was the familiar narrator, replaced by a teenage boy, and half the book was written in the third person, a new approach for the author. It received a warm critical reception, both in Japan and abroad, and became another international bestseller.

The story is told through two parallel narratives. In the first, a teenage boy has run away from his father’s home to try to find his estranged mother and sister. We do not learn his real name but he calls himself Kafka, after his favourite writer. During his journey he frequently interacts with his alter ego, or imaginary friend, Crow. He ends up in a remote private library in Takamatsu, run by the mysterious Miss Saeki, and is permitted to stay. There he befriends Oshima, a transgender librarian. The two of them meet and discuss life and literature every day. It is, for Kafka, an idyllic existence until the police arrive, looking to question him about a violent crime.

The second narrative concerns Nakata, an elderly man considered slow and simple. As a child he was involved in a curious incident while on a school trip. His entire class was rendered unconscious following a bright flash of light in the sky. Nakata took longer to recover than his classmates and, when he did come round, had lost his memory but gained the ability to communicate with cats. Now, in his old age, he makes a small living by using his unusual talent to find lost cats in the neighbourhood. What better way to find a cat than by asking other cats? One such search takes Nakata, like Kafka, on a road trip that will change his life.

Through this off-kilter plot Murakami explores themes of identity—Oshima’s transgender character, is Miss Saeki Kafka’s long-lost mother?, are Kafka and Nakata facets of the same person?—and destiny, Kafka’s storyline owing more than a passing nod to the Oedipus myth. But the author himself has always deflected any analysis of his novels, they are to be enjoyed as pure stories with no need to read anything into them.

I interviewed Murakami prior to publication of Kafka on the Shore and asked him about the change of direction the book represented: ‘Most of my fiction up till now has been first person narratives dealing with a male protagonist (and narrator) in his twenties or thirties. A few years ago, however, I started taking a different direction and began writing in a different voice and perspective. There’s something about that style of writing that suits me. In any case, whenever I write a new novel I decide on a theme and attempt to do something new and different.’

However ‘new and different’ each novel may be, readers will be able to spot recurring themes and motifs across Murakami’s work. Cats appear in almost every book he writes, and play a particularly important role in Kafka on the Shore. Classical and jazz music provide the background score to many scenes. And then there is the otherworldly, the parallel universes, the sense that something is just a little out of whack with the world. Kafka on the Shore arguably represents the culmination of all these themes; it features all the elements of classic Murakami and yet does, as the author intended, offer us something truly new and different.

I once asked Murakami what he thought of adaptations of his novels: ‘I make it a rule not to see film and stage versions of my works. When I write a work, I’m already creating a movie or play in my mind and don’t feel like watching someone else’s new movie or stage version.’

Fortunately, we do not have to follow his example.

May: In Review

I read some books this month. Here's what I thought of them.

Antoine Laurain - The President's Hat

Francois Mitterand leaves his hat behind after dining in a brasserie. A fellow diner picks it up and finds his luck turns for the better once he starts wearing it. That is, until he leaves it on a train and the hat's next owner succumbs to good fortune. A tremendously fun fable in which characters pass the narrative baton to each other in the form of the President's hat. Escapism with a little bit of bite to it.

Catherine Smith - The New Cockaigne

A funny and thought-provoking prose poem in which a new nihilist republic is declared, group sex is encouraged, pies rain from the sky and farting becomes a comeptitive sport. Made me chuckle more than once.

Terry Teachout - Duke: The Life of Duke Ellington

Ellington was the first great composer of the jazz age and Teachout's biography does a grand job of charting his career and giving us glimpses of the man. For me, it spent a bit too much time on band line-ups, recording history and accounts of concerts and not quite enough on trying to understand Ellington himself but it is still an impressive piece of work.

Carlos Labbé - Navidad & Matanza

This might be the story of a kidnapping: the teenage son and daughter of a wealthy software developer go missing in Chile. Or it might be the story of a journalist, years later, trying to work out what happened to the kids. Or, it could even be an account of a group of writers locked in cells composing a novel together as part of a scientific experiment, a novel about a kidnapping. Labbé has attempted something quite bold here, a brave experiment, but it doesn't quite work for me. It's a bit too sketchy, the novel comes in at under 100 pages, and the story doesn't really stick. Nice try though.

Chris F. Westbury - The Bride Stripped Bare by Her Bachelors, Even

Two blokes with OCD and a fascination with Marcel Duchamp go on an elaborate road trip, with the help of a friend they rope in to do the driving, to pick up a replica chocolate grinder that has a tentative link to a Duchamp piece called, you guessed it, The Bride Stripped Bare by Her Bachelors, Even. Weird and wonky but with a huge heart.

Richard Cowper - Worlds Apart

A very funny science fiction novel in which a bored suburban teacher creates a fantasy world that starts to encroach on his real life. Imagine Reggie Perrin written by Douglas Adams. I wrote a longer review earlier in the month.

Kent Haruf - Our Souls at Night

I rarely re-read a book so quickly, I first read this in January, but I wanted it fresh in my mind when I wrote my full review which I posted up here the other day. In short: a heartbreaking swansong.

Arthur Quiller-Couch - The Splendid Spur

Long-time readers of this blog will be aware of the affection I have for Q, as he was known. This Civil War adventure novel, written when he was still in his 20s, is not one of his finest works. Painted with broad strokes and with a fairly ridiculous plot, it should probably be filed away as 'juvenilia'.

Robert Williams - Into the Trees

A married couple move to a barn conversion in a remote forest because it is the only place their baby daughter will stop crying and sleep through the night. A shy, lonely man would rather work on a farm for low wages, staying in an old caravan, than live in his decaying house in town. An abusive husband gets involved in criminal activity in an attempt to stop his wife hassling him to find a job. These lives get tangled together in the aforementioned forest in a compelling story. Williams has a no-frills narrative style which I warmed to immediately and I polished this off in a day. The book was a loan from novelist Ray Robinson who rates it highly and I can see why.

Fred Uhlman - Reunion

There aren't many books you can polish off over breakfast but this, coming in at 82 pages including introduction and afterword, is one of them. There also aren't many books that go from good to great in their final sentence, but this is one of them too. Two teenage boys, practically young men, become friends at school in 1930s Germany. One is Christian, the son of a Count, the other, our narrator, is the son of a Jewish doctor. The Nazi Party is on the rise and you won't be surprised with how the main thrust of this story pans out, although it is handled with great skill and the development of the friendship is exquisitely done, but the ending is unexpected and elevates what could have been a very good novella into something altogether more special. A word of warning, however, do not read the introduction first as it is a spoiler-ridden annoyance.

Coco Moodysson - Never Goodnight

Although I am delighted to be setting up my new imprint, Aardvark Bureau, I do feel a bit sad when I see finished copies of Friday Project books that I signed up before I left HarperCollins. One such book is this Swedish graphic novel about three young girls who try to form a punk band. It was recently made into the movie We are the Best. Unlike all the other books I have published I wasn't able to read this one before I signed it because I don't speak Swedish so I took great pleasure in sitting down to enjoy this translation. It is really rather good and I hope it does well when released later in June.

So there you have it. The month, in brief.

The Last and Saddest Story

For many years on these pages I have been championing the work of Kent Haruf. I have described him as a poet of the precious ordinary, a writer who can capture the essence of life, of love, of a whole community in simple, direct prose. Each of his novels is a quiet masterpiece.

Our Souls at Night is his final book. Diagnosed with a terminal illness, he was determined to write one more novel. He did so quickly and the result is a short, sparse and thoughtful work.

It is also, perhaps, the most sadly beautiful story I have ever read.

Addie Moore and Louis Waters live opposite each other. They are both eldery. They both lost their spouses some years before. Addie has a proposal to make.

I wonder if you would consider coming to my house sometimes to sleep with me.

What? How do you mean?

I mean we are both alone. We've been by ourselves for too long. For years. I'm lonely. I think you might be too. I wonder if you would come and sleep in the night with me. And talk.

He stared at her, watching her, curious now, cautious.

You don't say anything. Have I taken your breath away? she said.

I guess you have.

I'm not talking about sex.

I wondered.

And so it begins. Louis turns up at night, with his toothbrush and pajamas, and the two of them lie in bed, talk and hold hands, and eventually fall asleep. They talk of their children, their marriages, their mistakes and, once the neighbours begin to notice, how to handle the gossip of a small town. A friendship, somewhat romantic, starts to grow.

But then Addie's grandson comes to stay while his parents attempt to repair their faulty marriage and this new thing, this fragile and beautiful new thing they have been building is tested for strength.

I confidently predict that Our Souls at Night will be one of the books of the year for critics and readers alike. It will move many, and make a fair few of them cry. It is a book about ageing, a book about acceptance. It is heartwarming and heartbreaking in equal measure.

Kent Haruf died in November of last year, just two days after the final edit of his book was complete.

Is This the Forgotten Book That Inspired Douglas Adams?

I was in the middle of reading a shedload of submissions for Aardvark Bureau and needed a break. I wanted to read an actual physical book, but I didn't have time for a long one. I picked Worlds Apart by Richard Cowper from my shelves pretty much at random.

By the end of the opening page I knew I had chosen well.

Although the next page was a bit of a surprise.


But once I had finished page three I was pretty sure I had read something like this before.

Not exactly like this, no, but certainly similar. A similar tone. A similar sense of humour. That mention of a spiral arm of the galaxy. The lone human being aware of some great revelation. It was a bit, well, a bit Douglas Adams. A bit Hitchhiker's.

The story that unfolds is actually more akin to Reggie Perrin than Arthur Dent. George Cringe is a bored husband with a bored wife and bored kids. He lives in a boring town and is bored with his job as a teacher. His only escapes are fantasies about a young teaching student called Jennifer and the science fiction novel he writes in his spare time.

That novel is set on the planet Agenor in the galaxy of Chnas. The inhabitants resemble humans in some ways but have a higher state of consciousness and enjoy a more cerebral version of sex, something they call grokking. The central characters are Zil and Orgypp, who seem to be a couple. Zil is writing a story called Shorge Gringe's Pilgrimage set on a planet called Urth.

Which is where things get really interesting. Is George real and Zil a fictional character he has created? Or are things the other way round? Or perhaps they both exist? Their two realities start to overlap, with scenes and images and dialogue crossing over between the two, so the reader becomes increasingly unsure. George's fumbling attempts to seduce Jennifer seem real enough, but then so does Zil's sudden appearance in Bagshot Road.

Perhaps we can never know for sure, and that is half the fun. World's Apart is not to be taken too seriously even if it does, at times, capture 1970s society quite brilliantly in political asides disguised as comedy. It also mocks the SF scene, with an amusing set piece at a SF convention.

But what about the Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy comparisons? Did they end after page three?

Not quite.

At several points I was reminded of Douglas Adams. Little one liners. Observations. The way many Chnassian scenes were introduced felt a bit like the Guide speaking an entry: The true natures of Space and Time have been the playthings of terrestrial philosophers since long before the Greeks invented the concept of logic and began sticking labels on everything, or, In Chnas it is a rule hallowed by antiquity that no two dwellings standing within sight of one another shall look alike. Imagine those sentences narrated by Peter Jones.

But all of this is pure conjecture. I was aware while I was reading it that I was looking for similarities and you can probably find them anywhere if you try hard enough. What I needed was one scene, one concept, one thing that I could directly link the the Hitchiker's universe.

And then the Drypidons came along.

The Drypidons are underwater creatures—'in appearance they bore a close resemblance to terrestrial dolphins'—with superior intelligence. They have their own planet within the Chnas universe and, at one point, Zil takes a party of Agenorian schoolkids on a trip to visit them. There, one Drypidon tells Zil about the 'overlap', a phenomenon in which creatures from Chnas are transported to Urth. It seems that 650,000 Dyrpidon's were 'lost' to Urth that way in the past.

Hugely intelligent dolphins on Earth that are actually aliens? That pretty much did it for me.

I am not offering this as conclusive proof but I am willing to bet that Douglas Adams was aware of this book and may well have read it before writing The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy.  Worlds Apart was published in 1974, Hitchhiker was first broadcast in 1978. Cowper, the pen name of John Middleton Murry Jr, was a popular SF writer throughout the 1970s. Never a huge bestseller, he was nonetheless well known in SF circles and I find it unlikely that Adams would not have heard of him.

Am I right? We may never know. Does it matter? Probably not. I was delighted, though, to discover this funny, largely forgotten novel and hope my deranged ramblings above may have prompted a few of you to give it a go.

Worlds Apart by Richard Cowper, along with most of his other novels, was reissued on ebook a couple of years ago. Secondhand print editions are pretty easy to track down too. If you do read it, I'd love to hear your thoughts.
